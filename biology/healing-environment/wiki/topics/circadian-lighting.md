# Circadian Lighting in Healthcare Settings

> **Summary:** Adjusting light spectrum and intensity to match circadian rhythms (bright/blue-shifted during day, dim/warm at night) improves sleep quality, reduces falls, and supports immune function in hospital and rehabilitation settings. The 50% fall reduction finding is the most cited clinical outcome.

## Tier 1 Evidence

**Fall reduction.** [MDPI 2025](../papers/mdpi-circadian-2025.md): circadian-appropriate lighting → 50% fewer falls in hospital setting vs standard lighting. Secondary outcome: improved sleep quality.

**Surgical prehab sleep effect.** Light environment mediates surgical outcome partly through sleep quality ([UCLA 2025](../papers/ucla-prehab-2025.md)).

## Mechanism

The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) receives melanopsin-expressing retinal ganglion cell input, primarily sensitive to ~480 nm (blue-shifted) light. Suppressing this input after dusk normalizes melatonin onset → improved sleep architecture. Sleep is the primary anabolic state for tissue repair — circadian disruption directly extends healing timelines.

In hospital settings, conventional lighting maintains high blue-spectrum illumination 24/7, chronically suppressing melatonin and disrupting sleep.

## Clinical Implementation

- Blue-rich (5000-6500K) light during morning/day hours
- Warm (2700-3000K), dimmed light in evening
- Blackout capability during sleep hours
- Minimal nighttime light for nursing checks (red-shifted preferred, <10 lux)

Key contrast: standard hospital lighting is typically 4000K, ~200-300 lux, maintained through evening. This is chronobiologically damaging.
