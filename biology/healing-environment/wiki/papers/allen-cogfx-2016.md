# Allen et al. 2016 — Harvard COGfx Study

**Authors:** Joseph G. Allen, Piers MacNaughton, Usha Satish, Suresh Santanam, Jose Vallarino, John D. Spengler  
**Journal:** Environmental Health Perspectives  
**DOI:** 10.1289/ehp.1510037  
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4892924/  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1289/ehp.1510037

## Summary

Office workers in enhanced ventilation + low-VOC environments scored 101% higher on standardized cognitive function tests vs conventional office environments. One of the clearest demonstrations that indoor air quality directly affects cognitive performance.

## Key Findings

- **N=24** office workers, double-blind crossover design
- Three conditions: Conventional, Enhanced (40 cfm/person outdoor air), Enhanced + low-VOC
- Enhanced vs Conventional: +61% cognitive function
- Enhanced + low-VOC vs Conventional: +101% cognitive function
- **Crisis Response subscale**: +131%
- **Strategy subscale**: +288%
- CO2 (higher in conventional) and VOCs independently associated — effect requires BOTH reduced VOC and adequate ventilation

## Important Nuance

The "doubling ventilation → 101% improvement" shorthand misattributes the effect to ventilation alone. The full finding: enhanced ventilation + low-VOC conditions. VOC reduction is equally important. In a conventional building with good ventilation but high VOC (from furniture, flooring, cleaning products), effects will be attenuated.

## Implications for Rehabilitation Settings

If cognitive function is impaired by poor air quality, patient engagement with therapy (CBT, physiotherapy, occupational therapy) is compromised. Baseline air quality is a prerequisite for therapeutic claims.
