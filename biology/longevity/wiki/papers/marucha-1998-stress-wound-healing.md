# Stress impairs wound healing 40% (within-subjects)

## Summary
Marucha, Kiecolt-Glaser, and Favagehi within-subjects study:
**n=11 dental students**. Each subject received two 3.5-mm punch
biopsies on the hard palate at two timepoints — one during summer
vacation, one **three days before a major examination**. Each
student was their own control.

**Exam-period wounds healed 40% slower** (about 3 extra days to
complete healing, p < 0.01). **IL-1β mRNA expression dropped
68%** at exam-period wounds vs. vacation-period wounds, providing
a mechanistic immunological link.

**Every single student** healed faster and produced more IL-1β
mRNA during the vacation timepoint than during the exam
timepoint — i.e., zero crossover.

## Why it matters
The cleanest small-N within-subjects demonstration that
psychological stress measurably impairs wound healing through a
specific immune mechanism (IL-1β). Frequently cited as evidence
that stress reduction matters for clinical outcomes that depend on
tissue repair (post-surgical recovery, infection resistance,
chronic wound healing).

## Caveats
- **Small N (11)**, single demographic (young, healthy dental
  students), highly specific stressor (exam week). Generalising
  to chronic life stress, sleep deprivation, or older
  populations is by mechanism, not by direct evidence.
- Endpoint is wound healing, not mortality or biological aging.
- Mucosal wound is a useful biomarker but not necessarily
  representative of all tissue-repair processes.
- The within-subjects design is the trial's main strength:
  removes between-subjects confounders entirely.

## Practical takeaway
- Stress measurably blunts a specific component of immune
  function (IL-1β-driven inflammation needed for healing). Not
  speculation; mechanism is biochemically demonstrated.
- For longevity purposes, this anchors stress-reduction
  interventions to a real biological substrate, but the wiki
  positions them as supportive, not high-leverage.
