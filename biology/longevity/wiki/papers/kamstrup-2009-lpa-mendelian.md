# Lp(a) is causal for MI: Mendelian randomization across 3 cohorts

## Summary
Kamstrup et al. combined Mendelian randomization across three
Copenhagen white-population cohorts:

| Cohort | Design | N | MI events |
|--------|--------|---|-----------|
| Copenhagen City Heart Study | Prospective, 16-yr follow-up | 8,637 | 599 |
| Copenhagen General Population Study | Cross-sectional | 29,388 | 994 |
| Copenhagen Ischemic Heart Disease Study | Case-control | 2,461 | 1,231 |

LPA gene KIV-2 copy-number variants and SNPs that **causally elevate
Lp(a)** also elevated MI risk: HRs of ~1.6–2.6 across the highest
Lp(a)-genotype categories vs. lowest. Because the genetic instrument
is fixed at conception and cannot be confounded by lifestyle or
prior disease, this establishes Lp(a) as **causal**, not merely
correlational, for myocardial infarction.

## Why it matters
Lp(a) is the most common genetic CVD risk factor (~20% of the
general population has elevated levels) and is **independent of
LDL-C and ApoB**. Standard lipid panels do not measure it. Diet,
exercise, and statins do not lower it meaningfully. Until recently,
there was no targeted Lp(a)-lowering therapy. Kamstrup 2009 is the
foundational genetic evidence that lowering Lp(a) should reduce
MI — which in turn motivated the current generation of antisense
and siRNA therapies (pelacarsen, olpasiran, lepodisiran) now in
phase 3.

## Key findings
- Highest vs lowest LPA genotype: ~2× MI risk.
- Effect attenuates only modestly after adjustment for LDL-C,
  HDL-C, BP, BMI, smoking — i.e., Lp(a) acts independently.
- Mendelian-randomization design rules out reverse causation and
  most lifestyle confounding.

## Caveats
- Population is European-ancestry Danish; genotype-phenotype
  relationships for Lp(a) differ across ancestries (East Asian
  populations have lower Lp(a) on average and different LPA-allele
  effect sizes).
- Mendelian randomization assumes no horizontal pleiotropy; for
  Lp(a) this assumption is well-supported because LPA expression
  is largely liver-specific.
- Establishes causality but does not directly establish that
  pharmacologically lowering Lp(a) will reduce MI. That is the
  question phase-3 Lp(a)-lowering trials (HORIZON, OCEAN(a)) are
  designed to answer (readouts pending; see
  [[analysis/yet-to-publish]]).

## Practical takeaway
- **Get an Lp(a) measurement once in your life** if you have not
  already. It is set largely by genetics and changes little with
  lifestyle. A single measurement is sufficient barring major
  inflammation or unusual physiological state.
- Reference lab cutoffs vary; >50 mg/dL or >125 nmol/L is broadly
  considered elevated, with risk gradient continuing to higher
  values.
- If elevated and you have other risk factors, that is a stronger
  case for aggressive ApoB lowering and other modifiable-risk
  optimisation while Lp(a)-targeted therapies await regulatory
  approval.
- **Avoid niacin** as a putative Lp(a) lowerer; it modestly lowers
  Lp(a) but multiple RCTs (AIM-HIGH, HPS2-THRIVE) showed no
  cardiovascular benefit and increased adverse events.
