# Negative result: plasmapheresis does not reduce epigenetic age in healthy adults

## Summary
Healthy adults underwent **8 or 4 plasmapheresis sessions over 18 weeks**,
measuring biochemical, hematological, and **multi-clock epigenetic
aging** parameters (Steve Horvath co-authored). Lipid profile improved.
**No significant epigenetic rejuvenation observed**; instead aging clock
measurements indicated **acceleration** in DNAmGrimAge, the Hannum clock,
and DunedinPACE. Authors conclude the protocol "requires further
refinement" and flag long-term safety concerns.

## Why it matters
A **negative result** that disciplines the parabiosis-derived hype
around plasma exchange. Direct counterweight to [[papers/tpe-ivig-biological-age-rct-2025]],
which found rejuvenation only when plasma exchange was **combined with
IVIG replacement**. Together the two trials suggest the active
ingredient is not "remove old factors" but "introduce something
specific" (immunoglobulins or another component of replacement fluid).

## Key findings
- 8 or 4 plasmapheresis sessions over 18 weeks; healthy donors.
- Reduced cholesterol and lipid markers (consistent with lipoprotein
  removal).
- **No reduction** in any first-generation or second-generation
  epigenetic clock vs. baseline.
- **Acceleration** in DNAmGrimAge, Hannum, DunedinPACE (effect size
  small but directionally negative).
- One mild allergic reaction across procedures; no serious AE.

## Caveats
- Healthy-donor cohort: protocol is a blood-donation paradigm, not a
  full therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE) with replacement fluid.
- Power: relatively small N for clock-level conclusions.
- Selection: clock acceleration may reflect transient inflammatory
  response from the procedure itself.
