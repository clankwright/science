# IV iPSC-derived young immune cells rescue brain aging

## Summary
Cedars-Sinai / Svendsen lab. **Intravenous infusion** of **human
iPSC-derived induced mononuclear phagocytes (iMPs)** into **aged and
5xFAD AD mice** restored **hippocampal mossy cells**, improved
**hippocampus-dependent cognition**, reduced **age-elevated serum
amyloid proteins (SAA2, SAP)**, and **reduced transcriptional age** in
9 of 15 hippocampal cell types according to an ML age estimator. The
infused cells **stay peripheral** — the rejuvenation effect is
**systemic and immune-mediated**, not local engraftment.

## Why it matters
A new modality: **off-the-shelf iPSC-derived young immune cells** as a
**cell-therapy senomorphic / rejuvenation product**. Distinct from
[[papers/adsc-exosomes-2022]] (extracellular vesicles), from
[[papers/senolytic-cart-upar-2024]] (engineered T cells targeting
senescent cells), and from autologous stem-cell approaches. iPSC source
means scalable, bankable manufacturing.

## Key findings
- IV-infused human iMPs improved cognition in aged and 5xFAD mice.
- Restored hippocampal mossy cells.
- Reduced age-elevated serum amyloids (SAA2, SAP).
- ML transcriptional age estimator: 9 of 15 hippocampal cell types
  rejuvenated.
- iMPs persist in periphery (not in CNS) — effect is systemic.

## Caveats
- Allogeneic human cells in mice: persistent engraftment limited.
- Mechanism (which secreted factor / immune signal) not yet identified.
- Clinical translation will require human safety + GMP iMP manufacture
  scale-up.
