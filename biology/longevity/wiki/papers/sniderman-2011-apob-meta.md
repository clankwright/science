# apoB beats LDL-C as a CV risk marker (head-to-head meta-analysis)

## Summary
Sniderman et al. meta-analysis of published epidemiological studies
comparing **LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and apolipoprotein B (apoB)** as
predictors of cardiovascular risk. Head-to-head: **apoB had the
strongest relative-risk reduction (RRR 1.43)**, non-HDL-C
intermediate (RRR 1.34), LDL-C the weakest (RRR 1.25). apoB beat
non-HDL-C by 5.7%, beat LDL-C by 12.0%. Authors estimated that
over 10 years, an apoB-based prevention strategy would prevent
**500,000 more cardiovascular events** than a non-HDL-C strategy.

## Why it matters
Underpins the modern recommendation to measure **apoB rather than
LDL-C** when available. apoB counts every atherogenic particle
(LDL, VLDL, IDL, Lp(a), remnants) directly; LDL-C is a calculated
estimate of cholesterol content in the LDL subset. Discordance
between apoB and LDL-C is common, and apoB wins every time the two
disagree. The 2019 ESC/EAS dyslipidaemia guidelines now name apoB as
the more accurate marker.

## Key findings
- Meta-analysis across published epidemiological studies.
- apoB RRR for CV events: 1.43 (95% CI 1.35-1.51).
- non-HDL-C RRR: 1.34 (95% CI 1.24-1.44).
- LDL-C RRR: 1.25 (95% CI 1.18-1.33).
- apoB superior to non-HDL-C by 5.7%, to LDL-C by 12.0%
  (both p < 0.001).
- Estimated 10-yr event prevention: apoB strategy prevents 500,000
  more events than non-HDL-C in the US.

## How to act on it
- Add **apoB** to standard lipid panels at routine bloodwork
  (~$10-20 added cost; included in many "longevity panels").
- Useful targets:
  - apoB <80 mg/dL for primary prevention.
  - apoB <60 mg/dL for high-risk / secondary prevention.
- Discordance with LDL-C (high apoB but normal LDL-C) is the
  signal-to-act case.

## Caveats
- 2011 paper; methodology has been debated. The Marston 2022 JAMA
  Cardiology Mendelian-randomization analysis strengthens the
  causal inference for apoB.
- Non-HDL-C is a reasonable second-best when apoB is unavailable.
