# Window view (trees vs brick wall) and surgical recovery

## Summary
Ulrich's classic *Science* paper: retrospective matched-pair
analysis of cholecystectomy recovery records from a suburban
Pennsylvania hospital, 1972–1981. **n=23 patients with rooms
overlooking trees vs n=23 matched controls with rooms overlooking a
brick wall** (matched on sex, age, smoking, weight, year of
surgery, floor level).

Tree-view patients had:
- **Shorter hospital stay (7.96 vs 8.70 days).**
- **Fewer negative evaluative comments** in nursing notes
  ("upset", "needs encouragement").
- **Fewer doses of potent analgesics** (and more of weak/oral
  analgesics) on post-op days 2–5.

## Why it matters
The most-cited single study underpinning the "biophilic design"
literature and a foundational citation for hospital-architecture
recommendations on natural light, nature views, and circadian
lighting. Effect sizes are modest in absolute terms (~0.74 day
shorter stay) but consistent across multiple endpoints.

## Caveats
- **Retrospective design with non-random room assignment.** The
  matched-pair analysis controls for measured confounders, but
  unmeasured factors (which patients were assigned to which
  rooms by ward staff, who has more visitors, who is in better
  baseline psychological state) cannot be ruled out.
- **Small N (23 pairs)** in a single hospital, single procedure,
  in 1972–1981 nursing/surgical-recovery culture; 1980s post-op
  pain management and length of stay practices differ from
  current standards.
- The result has not been replicated in modern randomized form
  with the same effect size; subsequent biophilic-design
  literature is largely observational and small-N.
- Distinct from longevity per se: this is a healing-environment
  study with a recovery endpoint, not a mortality or
  biological-aging endpoint. Included here because Reddit
  commenter feedback flagged the built-environment dimension as a
  gap, and Ulrich is the most-cited primary source.

## Practical takeaway
- Effects of natural views, daylight, and reduced ambient noise
  on patient recovery are real but small relative to the
  high-leverage interventions in the rest of the wiki.
- For longevity decisions, the built-environment literature is
  context, not a primary lever.
- For hospital design or post-surgical-recovery decisions, this
  literature is more directly relevant; the wiki is not the right
  reference.
