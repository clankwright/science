# Global MASLD prevalence 30%, rising fast; mortality is mostly cardiovascular

## Summary
Younossi et al. systematic review and meta-analysis: **92 studies,
n=9,361,716** total participants. Reports current global
prevalence and mortality of nonalcoholic fatty liver disease
(NAFLD), now reclassified as **metabolic dysfunction-associated
steatotic liver disease (MASLD)**.

**Global MASLD prevalence: 30.05%** (95% CI 27.88–32.32). Up from
25.26% in 1990–2006 to **38.00% in 2016–2019** — roughly a 50%
relative rise in three decades.

Regional prevalence: highest in Latin America (44.4%) and the
Middle East/North Africa (36.5%); lowest in Western Europe
(25.1%). The US ranges 30–40% depending on the cohort.

**Mortality among MASLD cohorts** (per 1,000 person-years):
- All-cause: 12.60
- **Cardiac: 4.20** (~33% of all-cause)
- Liver-specific: 0.92 (~7% of all-cause)

Cardiovascular causes dominate MASLD mortality. Liver-specific
death (cirrhosis, HCC) is the minority.

## Why it matters
MASLD is now the most common chronic liver disease worldwide and
is tightly coupled to the metabolic-syndrome cluster (obesity,
T2D, dyslipidemia). Most MASLD patients die of cardiovascular
disease, not liver disease — which makes MASLD effectively a
**downstream signal of cardiometabolic dysfunction** for longevity
purposes. A liver fat reading is a biomarker of overall metabolic
trajectory, not primarily a hepatology problem.

## Key findings
- Global prevalence 30%, rising; ~38% in 2016–2019 cohorts.
- Regional gradient: lowest in Western Europe (~25%), highest in
  Latin America (~44%).
- All-cause mortality 12.60/1,000 person-years; cardiac mortality
  is ~4× liver-specific mortality.
- The same metabolic-syndrome interventions
  ([[papers/pischon-2008-waist-mortality]]) that move waist /
  visceral fat / glucose are upstream of MASLD reversal.

## Caveats
- Heterogeneity across studies in MASLD definition (ultrasound vs
  MRI vs biopsy). Older studies often relied on imaging-based
  diagnosis.
- "MASLD" terminology change (from NAFLD) was finalised in 2023;
  some studies in this meta-analysis predate the criteria.
- Mortality numbers are pooled from cohorts with mixed severity;
  not stratified by fibrosis stage in this summary.
  (Fibrosis stage is the strongest predictor of liver-specific
  mortality but a weaker predictor of all-cause mortality compared
  with overall metabolic burden.)
- Observational; reverse-causation between MASLD and CVD is
  partly bidirectional given shared upstream drivers.

## Practical takeaway
- An ALT or AST chronically above the upper-normal limit, or a
  routine ultrasound noting "fatty liver", is a meaningful
  cardiometabolic signal — not just a liver-specific concern.
- The intervention is not "treat the liver"; it is the same set
  of upstream interventions in Tier A of `recommendations.md`:
  weight loss, fibre, fitness, alcohol minimisation,
  glucose/insulin management. GLP-1s now have direct evidence in
  MASH ([[papers/select-2023-semaglutide-cv-outcomes]] is for
  CVD, not liver, but the GLP-1 class also has phase-3 MASH data
  that are positive on hepatic outcomes).
- Vitamin E 800 IU/day has historically been recommended for
  biopsy-proven NASH in non-diabetic adults (PIVENS trial).
  Fish oil and Mediterranean diet have small RCT evidence for
  reducing liver fat. Liver-specific pharmacology (resmetirom,
  approved 2024) is for advanced fibrosis, not lifestyle-
  manageable steatosis.
