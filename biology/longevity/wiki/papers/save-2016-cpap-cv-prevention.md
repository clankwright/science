# SAVE: CPAP did not reduce CV events in moderate-severe OSA

## Summary
McEvoy et al. **SAVE** (Sleep Apnea cardioVascular Endpoints)
multicentre RCT: **n=2,687 patients with moderate-severe
obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) and prior cardiovascular or
cerebrovascular disease**, randomised 1:1 to **CPAP + usual care**
or usual care alone. Mean follow-up 3.7 years; mean CPAP adherence
3.3 hr/night.

**Primary composite endpoint** (CV death + MI + stroke +
hospitalisation for unstable angina, HF, or TIA): **17.0% CPAP vs
15.4% control, HR 1.10 (95% CI 0.91–1.32, p=0.34).** **Primary
endpoint NOT met.**

CPAP improved subjective sleepiness, snoring, mood, and
quality-of-life secondary endpoints. It did not reduce hard
cardiovascular outcomes.

## Why it matters
Disrupts a widely-held assumption that treating OSA cuts
cardiovascular risk in patients with both. SAVE was designed
specifically to test that assumption in the secondary-prevention
population where the rationale was strongest. Result is null at
the primary-endpoint level. The likely explanation, supported by
post-hoc subgroup analyses, is **inadequate CPAP adherence** (mean
3.3 hr/night, well below the ~6 hr/night threshold where
mechanistic benefit is plausible). Patients with >4 hr/night
adherence had nominally better outcomes, but that comparison is
non-randomised and confounded by adherence-as-health-marker.

For longevity readers, this is a corrective to the "treat your
sleep apnea or you'll die" framing. CPAP is excellent for daytime
sleepiness, snoring partner-friction, and quality of life. Its
cardiovascular benefit has not been demonstrated in RCT.

## Key findings
- Primary composite HR 1.10 (CI 0.91–1.32, p=0.34).
- Secondary endpoints (snoring, daytime sleepiness, mood, QoL)
  significantly improved on CPAP.
- Mean CPAP adherence 3.3 hr/night; nominal benefit suggested at
  >4 hr/night in non-randomised comparison.
- AHI on CPAP: median 3.7 events/hr (well-controlled OSA).

## Caveats
- **Adherence interpretation is the central debate.** SAVE
  enrolled real-world patients; per-protocol benefit at high
  adherence cannot be cleanly separated from healthy-adherer bias.
- The trial does not address: (a) primary-prevention populations
  without prior CVD, (b) patients with severe OSA (AHI > 30) and
  resistant hypertension, (c) cognitive endpoints, (d) all-cause
  mortality with longer follow-up.
- For symptomatic OSA, CPAP remains first-line therapy on
  symptom and QoL grounds. SAVE does not change that
  recommendation.

## Practical takeaway
- If you have moderate-severe OSA with daytime sleepiness or
  cognitive impact, treat it for those reasons; CPAP is highly
  effective there.
- Do not start CPAP "for cardiovascular prevention" alone; the
  RCT-level evidence does not support that indication.
- Weight loss is the highest-leverage upstream intervention for
  OSA: 10% body-weight reduction can reduce AHI by 30–50% and
  may eliminate need for CPAP entirely in mild–moderate disease.
  GLP-1 weight-loss agents will likely move this further as their
  use scales.
