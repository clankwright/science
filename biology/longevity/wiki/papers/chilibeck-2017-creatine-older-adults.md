# Creatine + resistance training in older adults: meta-analysis

## Summary
Chilibeck et al. meta-analysis of **22 RCTs (n=721)** of creatine
monohydrate plus resistance training vs. resistance training alone
in older adults (mean age 57+, range up to ~80). Creatine added
**+1.37 kg lean tissue mass** (95% CI 0.97–1.76; p<0.00001) over
the training intervention, with significantly larger gains in
chest-press and leg-press 1RM. Trial duration 7–52 weeks; doses
mostly 5 g/day or weight-adjusted ~0.1 g/kg/day, with or without
loading phase.

## Why it matters
The wiki cites lean mass and strength as biomarkers strongly
predictive of mortality
([[papers/leong-2015-pure-grip-strength]],
[[papers/garcia-hermoso-2018-strength-mortality]],
[[papers/saeidifard-2019-resistance-mortality]]). Creatine moves the
same biomarkers as resistance training and the effect is additive on
top of training. By the same evidence logic that puts resistance
training in the actionable tier, creatine is the cleanest
supplement-aisle intervention with consistent meta-analysis support
on the relevant biomarkers in the relevant population.

## Key findings
- **+1.37 kg lean tissue mass** vs RT alone (95% CI 0.97–1.76,
  p<0.00001).
- Significantly larger 1RM gains on chest press and leg press
  (effect sizes consistent across studies).
- No serious adverse events. The creatine safety profile is well
  characterised: 5 g/day for years in healthy adults and up to
  30 g/day for 5 years in subset clinical populations
  (Kreider et al. 2017, *J Int Soc Sports Nutr* ISSN position
  stand, [PMID 28615996](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28615996/)).
- Effect present whether creatine was given before or after training
  sessions; loading-phase optional.

## Caveats
- Surrogate endpoint (lean mass, 1RM strength), not all-cause
  mortality. Inferred mortality benefit is by analogy to the
  resistance-training and grip-strength mortality literature, not
  directly demonstrated for creatine itself.
- Heterogeneity in trial design (creatine timing, loading vs no
  loading, RT protocol).
- Effects are modest. Creatine is an *adjunct* to resistance
  training, not a replacement; without progressive RT the gains
  are much smaller.
- Population is healthy or community-dwelling older adults; effects
  in frail/sarcopenic patients are extrapolated, not directly
  measured here.
- Some studies in the meta-analysis used non-monohydrate forms;
  creatine monohydrate remains the form with the deepest evidence
  base.

## Practical takeaway
- 5 g/day creatine monohydrate is the standard dose. Loading
  phases (20 g/day x 5–7 days) are optional and do not change the
  long-run outcome.
- Take with or after training; timing matters less than total
  daily intake.
- Mix into water or a meal; absorption is robust and not very
  food-dependent.
- Expect a 1–2 kg increase in lean body mass over a few months,
  partly water (intracellular) and partly muscle protein.
- Creatine is among the few supplement-aisle compounds with
  meta-analysis-level evidence on a biomarker the wiki has already
  validated as a mortality predictor. This is the asymmetry test
  highlighted in the [[analysis/evidence-tiers]] discussion: by
  the same standards that retain rapamycin (T3 mouse) as
  promising, creatine (T7 meta-analysis on validated biomarker)
  should not be dismissed.
