# Smart Telescopes

> **Summary:** Consumer telescopes with on-board AI for plate-solving, automatic tracking, and image stacking. The ZWO SeeStar S30/S50 ($500) was the budget breakthrough in 2023. Unistellar eVscope and Vaonis Vespera/Stellina partner with the SETI Institute and AAVSO so amateur observations contribute to professional exoplanet-transit timing and asteroid-occultation campaigns.

**Sources:** [[raw/amateur-science/astronomy-and-citizen-science.md]]

## What it does

Tap a target on a phone app; the telescope slews, plate-solves against the star catalog, locks tracking, and accumulates exposures. Outputs deep-sky images that previously required hours of manual setup. Higher-end units feed timing data into citizen-science campaigns.

## Access and cost

ZWO SeeStar S30 ~$500; SeeStar S50 ~$700. Vaonis Vespera Pro ~$2,500. Unistellar eVscope 2 ~$4,000. No subscription. Citizen-science participation is free.

## Distinctive trait

The combination of on-board AI for setup-free observing plus institutional partnerships that route amateur data into professional databases. A backyard observer with $2,500 in equipment contributes real data to SETI Institute exoplanet-transit timing and AAVSO variable-star records. Pre-2018 the same workflow required a $10k+ rig and a weekend of setup per night.

## Limits

- Limited aperture means deep-sky imaging plateaus quickly; not a replacement for serious astrophotography rigs.
- Citizen-science contributions require careful filtering; most amateur "exoplanet detections" are eclipsing binaries or pipeline artifacts.
- Light pollution still dominates; AI does not solve it.
- Subscription apps from some vendors gate advanced features.
