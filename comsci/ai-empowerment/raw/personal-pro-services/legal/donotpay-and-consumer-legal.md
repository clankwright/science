# DoNotPay and the consumer-legal cautionary tale

## DoNotPay
- Vendor: DoNotPay (Joshua Browder, founded 2015 to fight parking tickets).
- Pitch: "the world's first robot lawyer." Expanded over the years into contracts, divorce, refunds, lease disputes, immigration, etc.
- 2023 high-water mark: Browder offered $1M to anyone who would let an LLM argue a US Supreme Court case via earpiece (declined).
- Late 2023 onward: scrutiny escalated. Kathryn Tewson's investigation found the legal-document outputs were thin or wrong; lawyers found the contract analyses near-useless.

## FTC action and settlement
- Sept 25 2024: FTC announced charges as part of "Operation AI Comply." Complaint: DoNotPay marketed as a substitute for a human lawyer but had not tested whether outputs met that bar; never hired or retained attorneys to evaluate quality.
- Feb 2025 (final order): $193,000 monetary relief, 5-0 commission vote, must notify 2021-2023 subscribers about the FTC settlement, cannot advertise as equivalent to a human lawyer without supporting evidence.
- Significance: the first significant FTC enforcement against an AI legal-services consumer product. Sets the regulatory floor for "robot lawyer" claims going forward.

## Why DoNotPay matters for the wiki
- Cautionary tale, not a recommendation. The vendor that most aggressively claimed the consumer legal-AI use case got the loudest regulatory rebuke. The lesson is not "AI can't help with consumer legal questions" — frontier LLMs are demonstrably useful for that. The lesson is "marketing matters; UPL exposure is real; the substitute-for-a-lawyer framing draws fire."

## ChatGPT / Claude as paralegal substitute (the actual consumer story)
- The realistic 2026 path for a consumer with a legal question is not DoNotPay. It's:
  1. Paste the lease/contract/letter into ChatGPT or Claude.
  2. Ask: "explain this; what should I push back on; what's standard vs unusual; what is the risk of signing as written."
  3. Get a competent paralegal-tier explanation with no liability claim.
- Frontier LLMs handle: lease review, employment offer/severance review, reading EULAs and ToS, drafting demand letters, understanding small-claims procedure, navigating insurance disputes, decoding court filings sent to you, drafting (not filing) immigration forms, drafting wills (consumer-grade only — execution rules vary by state).
- They do *not* handle reliably: jurisdiction-specific statutory interpretation (varies state-to-state and country-to-country), live litigation strategy, anything where a missed deadline or incorrect filing would be malpractice if a lawyer did it.

## UPL (Unauthorized Practice of Law) status (2025-2026)
- All 50 US states make providing legal services without a license illegal. Penalties: fines, sometimes criminal.
- Open question: when ChatGPT answers "is this lease legal?" is OpenAI practicing law? UPL statutes were never written for software; rarely invoked against vendors.
- New York SB 7263 (introduced April 2025): would explicitly bar AI chatbot proprietors from giving "substantive" responses constituting unauthorized professional-services practice. Creates a private right of action. As of May 2026, advanced but not enacted. (Holland & Knight tracker, March 2026.)
- ABA / NCSC (Aug 2025 white paper): proposes modernizing UPL regs to *permit* vetted AI legal tools, with disclosure and data-security requirements. Regulatory sandbox model.
- Yale Journal of Law & Tech: "ChatGPT, Esq." argues UPL doctrine needs to be re-grounded for generative AI — not abandoned, but re-scoped.
- Consumer takeaway: using ChatGPT for your own lease review is fine. A startup wrapping ChatGPT and selling "AI lawyer" subscriptions is on thinner ice and should expect FTC + state-bar attention.
