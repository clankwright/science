# Evidence: do AI coaches actually change behavior?

## What the literature says

Honest summary: the 2024-2026 evidence base is thin, mostly self-report, and what RCTs exist show mixed results.

## Systematic reviews

A May 2025 Springer systematic review on AI-driven virtual assistants for physical activity promotion screened 2,849 studies. Findings: feasibility and acceptability are well-established; comparable efficacy to traditional digital interventions in some domains; insufficient evidence to claim AI assistants outperform conventional digital coaching on hard endpoints (steps, MVPA, weight, biomarkers). Most included studies were small, short (4-12 weeks), and used heterogeneous outcomes.

A 2025 PMC scoping review on human/AI/hybrid health coaching across 35 digital intervention studies found all coaching modalities feasible and acceptable, both human and AI showing positive impact on engagement and self-reported lifestyle outcomes, no clear superiority of AI.

A 2026 MDPI review on wearable biosensing + ML for training/coaching focused on sport-science applications. Strong evidence wearables can capture relevant physiological/biomechanical/biochemical markers; weak evidence the AI layer materially improves athletic outcomes vs a coach with the same data.

## Specific RCTs

- 2021 npj Digital Medicine: AI coaching intervention to increase physical activity in cancer survivors. Randomized, two AI conditions, modest positive effect on activity vs control during the intervention window. Effect persistence not measured.
- 2023 study: chatbot use coincided with increased motivation for walking and increased weekly steps and move minutes. Observational coincidence framing, not strict causal.
- 2024 RCT in older adults: chatbot vs control for MVPA. No significant difference in objective or subjective MVPA. Negative result in the population most often targeted by these tools.

## Vendor self-report numbers

- Oura Advisor beta: 60% reported helping understand metrics, 56% reported helping apply information. 60% used it weekly, 20% daily. Self-report from users who chose to engage.
- RISE: 80-83% report more energy within 5-7 days. Self-report, no control.
- Whoop Coach: members report actionable advice on personal patterns. No published outcome data.

These numbers are consistent with high engagement and high self-perceived value. They are not evidence of objective behavior change vs a control.

## The placebo problem

The category has a confound that's hard to design around. Wearing a Whoop and chatting with Coach makes you pay attention to sleep. Paying attention to sleep changes sleep behavior. Whether the AI added anything beyond the attention is the unanswered question. Most consumer-health interventions have this problem; the AI coach version is particularly susceptible because the visible product is conversation, not a measurable physical action.

## What an honest summary looks like

- Wearable + AI coach drives engagement up over wearable-alone (multiple sources, weak evidence).
- Engagement is correlated with behavior change in observational data.
- Causal claim that the AI specifically (vs a static rules-based app or a human) improves outcomes is unproven as of mid-2026.
- The most defensible claim: AI coaches lower the friction of accessing one's own data, which makes the data more likely to influence decisions. That is a real unlock without claiming it cures anything.

## Where to expect better evidence

- Eight Sleep + Autopilot has objective sleep-temperature actuation that could be RCT'd against fixed temp.
- TrainerRoad Adaptive Training has FTP gain data that's quantitative, though pre-LLM.
- CGM-based metabolic experiments (Stelo + AI coach) produce within-person quantifiable curves and are the easiest to design proper n=1 trials around.
