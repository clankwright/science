# Dyslexia and ADHD Tools

## What it is

Reading, writing, and executive-function tooling for cognitive accessibility (estimated 40M dyslexic adults and 15M ADHD adults in the US alone). Two big categories:

1. **Text-to-speech with high-quality AI voices** that make reading long-form text bearable: **Speechify**, **Voice Dream Reader**, **Audible AI narration** (2025 expansion), **NaturalReader**, **Read Aloud** (free Chrome extension).
2. **LLMs as executive-function scaffolds**: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini for task breakdown, summarization, drafting assistance, deadline planning, social-script generation. Often used informally; some specialized products (Goblin Tools, Saner.ai) build ADHD-aware UX on top.

## Specific unlocks

- **Speechify**: Apple Design Award 2025; built by founder with dyslexia and ADHD. Premium AI voices indistinguishable from human narration; word-by-word highlighting synced to audio (essential for dyslexic reading); reads PDFs, web pages, emails, scanned books. Speeds up to 9x normal. Mobile, web, browser extension. Heavy use among university students with documented learning disabilities (qualifies for accommodation funding).
- **Voice Dream Reader**: long-running iOS / Android favorite among blind and dyslexic users; integrates with Bookshare (DRM-free books for users with print disabilities). Bring-your-own-voice support including ElevenLabs and Apple Personal Voice.
- **Audible AI narration** (2025-2026): Audible expanded AI-narrated audiobooks meaningfully; titles previously not commercially audiobookable (mid-list, academic, niche) now have synthetic-voice editions for ~$1-5/title. Massively expands availability for dyslexic readers.
- **ChatGPT / Claude as executive-function scaffold**: tasks vague? Ask the model to break a project into 5 atomic steps with time estimates. Email feels overwhelming? Paste it and ask for a 1-line summary plus what you owe in reply. Calendar conflict? Paste and ask for a 3-option resolution. A 2023 ADDitude survey found ~70% of adult-ADHD ChatGPT users reported improvements in procrastination, task initiation, and communication confidence.
- **Goblin Tools** (free / $0.99 mobile): "Magic ToDo" auto-decomposes a vague task into sub-tasks at user-controlled "spiciness" (granularity); "Formalizer" rewrites blunt or harsh text into professional tone (and vice versa). Built explicitly for autistic and ADHD users.
- **Granola / Otter for ADHD professionals**: meeting auto-transcription and summary; the AI keeps the notes so the user can be present in the conversation rather than fighting to remember.
- **Bionic Reading / "Ambient Reading"**: bold-the-leading-letters technique, integrated into Speechify and Voice Dream; mixed evidence on efficacy but loved by some dyslexic users.
- **AI captioning of own speech for ADHD lecturers**: real-time transcription gives a fallback when the user loses the thread.

## Pre-AI baseline

- TTS quality before 2022 (eSpeak, Acapela, Ivona) was a strong barrier: robotic narration is itself fatiguing for dyslexic readers.
- Audiobooks were limited to traditionally-narrated titles ($15-30 each); Bookshare (free for documented print disabilities) had a substantial library but not everything.
- Executive-function support meant therapy ($150-300/session), coaching ($100-200/session), or self-help books that were ironically hard to read for dyslexic ADHD users.
- Task-breakdown apps (Todoist, TickTick) required the user to do the breakdown themselves; the cognitive load was the problem.
- Read Aloud and built-in TTS (macOS `say`, Windows Narrator) worked but sounded synthetic enough that long sessions were fatiguing.

## Cost / access

- **Speechify**: free tier (basic voices, limited features); Premium $11.58/mo or $139/yr. Often covered by university disability services.
- **Voice Dream Reader**: $20-30 perpetual (varies by platform).
- **ChatGPT**: free tier (GPT-4o usable amount); Plus $20/mo.
- **Claude**: free tier; Pro $20/mo.
- **Goblin Tools**: web free; iOS / Android $0.99.
- **Audible**: $14.95/mo membership.
- **Bookshare**: free for users with documented print disabilities (US + many countries).

## Maturity

**Production.** This is the most-mature accessibility AI category in 2026 because the underlying tech (TTS quality, LLM summarization) was the first to mature, and the use cases are not safety-critical (a wrong summary is annoying; a misread pill bottle is dangerous). Speechify alone reports >55M users. The hidden-disability angle matters: many users do not have a formal diagnosis but use these tools as functional accommodations.

Limits: dependence is real (some users report worsened executive function when AI tools become unavailable); LLM hallucination is a risk for academic / professional output; AI-generated text in school assignments runs into academic-integrity policies that often don't distinguish accessibility-tool use from cheating.
