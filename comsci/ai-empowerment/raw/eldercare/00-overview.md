# Eldercare augmentation and dementia memory aids overview

## The demographic situation

The Baby Boom generation begins hitting age 80 in 2026. In the US, 10% of all adults are now caregiving for a parent 65+, and another 3% for an aging spouse. Among adults with an aging parent, 24% identify as caregivers. Among lower-income adults with an aging parent or spouse, that share jumps to 39%. Women carry it disproportionately (28% vs 23% for men). Pew Research, Feb 2026.

Japan is the leading edge. Familial support ratio became the world's lowest in 2004. Its 2012 community-based integrated care system depends on a workforce that doesn't exist. Roughly 28% of Japanese over 65 live alone; the country is treated by Nature Aging (2025) as a template for what every other developed country sees within 15 years.

Europe varies. Italy, Germany, Spain are aging fast. The EU's informal caregiving labor pool has been quietly contracting since the 2010s; fewer kids, more migration, women in paid work.

Globally, home care services are 39.1% of total elderly care spend in 2026. Older people overwhelmingly want to stay home; this isn't a preference question, it's a cost-curve question. A nursing home bed in the US runs $9k-$12k/month. Aging in place runs $0-$5k/month depending on intensity.

## The family-caregiver gap

A US adult caring for a parent spends ~26 hours/week on caregiving on top of paid work. AARP estimates the unpaid care economy at $600 billion/year. The 2030 projection has the dependency ratio worsening every year through 2045. Paid in-home aides cost $30-40/hour, are in chronic short supply, and turn over at >70%/year.

Where the gap shows up:
- Adult children living 500+ miles from a parent, calling once a week, no idea if mom fell on Tuesday and lay there 14 hours.
- Spousal caregivers who themselves have a chronic condition, burning out at year 3-4.
- People with early dementia who could safely stay home with prosthetic memory and reminders, but get placed early because the family can't watch them 16 hours a day.
- Solo agers (no spouse, no kids, no nearby family) who are the fastest-growing cohort.

## What AI moves

This category is unusual: most "innovation" through 2023 was vaporware (Pepper-the-robot demos, vague "smart home for seniors"). 2024-2026 has produced things that actually move the needle:

1. **Audio/radar passive monitoring**: replaces "is mom OK?" with continuous signal. Sensi.AI, Cherish Serenity. No camera, no wearable. Detects falls, distress, atypical patterns.
2. **Proactive companion devices**: ElliQ NY rollout reported 95% loneliness reduction at 30 days, 60 interactions/day average. Real evidence, not vendor copy.
3. **AI scam call detection**: Pixel/Gemini Nano + Apple Call Screening. Catches AI voice-clone "family in distress" scams before $30k wires happen. FTC: $352M elder losses to AI scams in 2024 alone.
4. **Smarter fall detection**: AI-learned movement baselines (Kanega) cut false alarms; radar-based room-scale detection (Cherish) catches the soft falls wrist-worn devices miss.
5. **Voice-first interfaces**: Alexa/Nest as primary computer for non-tech-native elders. Bypasses the smartphone entirely.
6. **Medication adherence**: Hero/MedMinder dispensers + caregiver dashboards. Adherence is the single biggest preventable hospitalization driver.
7. **Memory aids**: face-recognition apps, lifelog review (SenseCam lineage), daily-routine narration. Genuinely contested evidence base but specific tasks work.
8. **Caregiver coordination**: Cariloop, Caribou, CareZone with LLM summarization. Lets a 5-sibling family stop CC'ing each other 40 emails/week.

## What it doesn't move (yet)

Generalist humanoid eldercare robots: still vapor through 2026. Toilet/transfer/bathing assist: not solved. Conversational agents that pass any reasonable Turing test for a person with mild cognitive impairment: borderline, with serious deception ethics issues.
