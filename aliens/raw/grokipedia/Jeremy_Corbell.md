# Jeremy Corbell

                                     Jeremy Kenyon Lockyer Corbell is an American investigative filmmaker, contemporary artist, and ufologist based in Los Angeles, California, best known for producing documentaries and releasing military footage related to unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP) and claims of government non-disclosure.
Corbell's career shifted from mixed-media art exhibitions and jiu-jitsu training to UFO investigations, with notable documentaries including *Bob Lazar: Area 51 & Flying Saucers* (2018), which examined physicist Bob Lazar's assertions of working on extraterrestrial craft at a classified site, and *Hunt for the Skinwalker* (2018), probing anomalous events at a Utah ranch. He has sourced and publicized several UAP videos from U.S. military insiders, such as infrared footage from 2019 depicting pyramid-shaped objects maneuvering near a Navy destroyer off San Diego, which the Pentagon later confirmed as authentic and unexplained. Additional releases include a 2020 disc-shaped object captured by ground forces and a spherical UAP descending toward the ocean, contributing to broader discussions on aerial anomalies despite lacking full official explanations.
As co-host of the *Weaponized* podcast alongside journalist George Knapp, Corbell analyzes whistleblower accounts and pushes for UAP data declassification, influencing congressional inquiries into potential national security threats from advanced technologies. His efforts have amplified empirical evidence from sensor data over anecdotal reports, yet he has drawn scrutiny for stylistic choices perceived as amplifying unverified elements to heighten public engagement, potentially undermining source trust in a field prone to hoaxes and institutional skepticism.
## Early Life and Martial Arts

### Childhood and Training Beginnings

Jeremy Corbell, born Jeremy Kenyon Lockyer Corbell on February 3, 1977, grew up as the middle child among three brothers. Little public information exists regarding his pre-adolescent years, though he later described an early immersion in physical discipline that shaped his worldview.
Corbell's formal training in martial arts commenced at age nine with jujitsu, marking the onset of a lifelong commitment to the discipline. This early start, around 1986 given his birth year, involved rigorous practice in traditional jujitsu techniques, fostering foundational skills in grappling, strikes, and self-defense. By his account, this period evolved into an intense dedication, positioning him as a "recovering martial arts addict" by adulthood.
### Competitive Achievements and Health Transition

Corbell attained the rank of black belt in traditional jujitsu, demonstrating proficiency in grappling and self-defense techniques. He developed a specialized discipline known as Quantum Jujitsu, which integrates conventional martial arts principles with philosophical and conceptual elements aimed at holistic personal development. For several years, he instructed jujitsu and yoga classes at the University of California, Santa Cruz, influencing students through practical training and inspirational teaching methods. No public records document competitive tournament participation or awards in martial arts events.
A pivotal health event altered Corbell's trajectory: he contracted a near-fatal respiratory disease that severely limited his physical capacity for rigorous training. This condition prevented sustained engagement in martial arts practice, prompting a reevaluation of his professional path. The transition facilitated his pivot to creative fields, including visual arts and documentary filmmaking, where physical demands were less intense.
## Artistic Career

### Development of Mixed-Media Style

Corbell's entry into visual art occurred in 2004 following a diagnosis of Valley fever, a fungal infection that rendered him unable to continue intensive martial arts training for over a year. During recovery, he initiated artistic creation as a therapeutic outlet, painting on vintage doors salvaged from demolitions in his Los Angeles childhood neighborhood. This foundational practice emphasized reclaimed materials, fostering an improvisational approach that integrated physical remnants of urban decay into expressive forms.
His mixed-media style evolved from these therapeutic origins into immersive installations by incorporating photography, newsprint, and layered assemblages. Early works utilized photographic techniques, such as shooting through a 1960s RediFlex camera overlaid with digital processing to produce distorted, hybrid images embedded in larger compositions measuring 24 by 24 inches. For instance, his 2010 series *ICON*, honoring Sharon Tate on the 40th anniversary of her death, blended satin prints, mixed-media canvases, and Polaroid installations with original negatives, merging historical iconography with contemporary fabrication. Similarly, *The Pope* incorporated clippings from *Los Angeles Times* articles on the Catholic Church's stem-cell research positions, layering textual archives with visual elements to critique institutional narratives.
This development reflected a self-described "accidental" progression, where Corbell's background in disciplined physical training informed a rigorous, multimedia experimentation unbound by formal academic training. Exhibitions like *Death to Life* and *Strange Love* further refined the style, emphasizing site-specific reclamation and fusion of fine art with performative elements akin to fashion and film projection, creating environments that engaged viewers multisensorially. The approach prioritized tactile, narrative-driven assemblages over conventional painting, drawing causal links between personal adversity and creative reinvention through empirical material experimentation.
### Key Exhibitions and Installations

Corbell's mixed-media exhibitions often incorporate recycled architectural elements, such as vintage doors and windows sourced from Los Angeles demolitions, embedded with contemporary materials like disassembled computer components to explore themes of transformation and obsolescence. One early installation series, "Death to Life," exemplified this approach by repurposing obsolete technology within historic frames, symbolizing the evolution from decay to renewal, and was displayed in select Los Angeles galleries and private venues.
A prominent exhibition, "Women in Sunglasses," garnered significant attention for its immersive presentation of female portraits shrouded in mystery and allure, blending photography, fashion elements, and sculptural components; it was showcased at avant-garde events in Los Angeles prior to 2010, establishing Corbell's reputation for provocative, multimedia displays.
Corbell's most extensive project, "ICON: Life Love & Style of Sharon Tate," debuted on August 9, 2009—the 40th anniversary of Tate's death—at High Profile Productions in Culver City, California, utilizing an 8,000-square-foot space to feature enlarged, hand-painted Polaroid images of a model clad in Tate's original wardrobe, including pieces by designers like Christian Dior and Yves Saint Laurent, framed in antique settings to emphasize her as a symbol of elegance rather than tragedy. The series, created with the Tate family's endorsement, comprised multiple parts such as large-format Polaroids, a "Blue Series," and resin-cast artifacts from Tate's era, initially mounted in resin-embedded 1960s windows. By the 50th anniversary in 2019, it expanded to a 350-piece collection celebrating Tate's life and cultural impact, with installations touring private residences and events, including a 2010 Oscar-week showing at a Chateau Marmont-adjacent home that benefited the Andre Sobel RIVER OF LIFE Foundation. This work highlighted Corbell's intent to reclaim narratives through fashion and visual reconstruction, drawing media coverage for reframing Tate's legacy beyond her victimization.
## Filmmaking Transition

### Motivations from Personal Challenges

Corbell's shift toward filmmaking stemmed from a severe respiratory illness he contracted while traveling in Nepal, which proved nearly fatal and permanently curtailed his ability to engage in the physically demanding world of competitive martial arts. This health crisis, occurring in his early adulthood, compelled a reevaluation of his career trajectory, redirecting his discipline and intensity from athletic competition to creative and investigative endeavors.
Amid recovery, Corbell acquired a camera that served as an instrumental tool—or "passport," as he described it—for exploring unresolved questions about unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP), a fascination ignited in 1989 by reports of advanced propulsion technologies. Lacking any formal film education, he leveraged this equipment to conduct interviews with eyewitnesses and insiders, viewing documentary production as a means to systematically probe extraordinary claims without the physical toll of prior pursuits. This pivot reflected a broader adaptation to his altered physical capacity, transforming personal limitation into a platform for truth-seeking inquiry into fringe topics dismissed by mainstream institutions.
The illness not only ended his martial arts dominance but also fostered resilience through alternative expressions, initially via mixed-media art before evolving into film as a dynamic medium for narrative control and public dissemination. Corbell has attributed this phase to a philosophical imperative: confronting the unknown directly, unhindered by institutional skepticism, to document credible testimonies that challenge conventional paradigms.
### Initial Projects and Techniques

Corbell's entry into filmmaking coincided with his pivot from visual arts and martial arts, channeling investigative curiosity toward documentaries on unexplained phenomena. His early project *Moonwalker* centers on Apollo 14 astronaut Edgar Mitchell, exploring Mitchell's advocacy for extraterrestrial intelligence, experiences with remote healing, and assertions of government concealment regarding non-human entities. Corbell conducted extended personal interviews with Mitchell, capturing discussions on topics including lunar alien bases and psychokinetic experiments, employing a direct-access method to foreground the subject's firsthand perspectives over external commentary.
The feature-length *Patient Seventeen*, released on October 10, 2017, marked Corbell's initial major documentary release, scrutinizing podiatrist Roger Leir's surgical removals of purported alien implants from 17 patients. The film documents Leir's procedures, subsequent metallurgical and isotopic analyses of the objects—which reportedly exhibited unusual compositions incompatible with terrestrial manufacturing—and Leir's interactions with skeptics, including challenges to authenticate the implants' origins. Corbell shadowed Leir during operations and consultations, integrating raw surgical footage, lab results from independent facilities, and interviews to construct a case for anomalous artifacts while highlighting evidentiary disputes.
In these works, Corbell adopted an investigative technique emphasizing primary sourcing: securing exclusive, unscripted access to protagonists like Mitchell and Leir, minimal directorial narration to avoid interpretive bias, and incorporation of scientific testing protocols to probe claims empirically. This cinéma vérité-inspired approach, drawing from his mixed-media background, prioritizes viewer-led assessment of testimonies and data—such as implant spectrometry showing iridium isotopes atypical for human tech—over sensational framing, though critics contend it amplifies unverified assertions from fringe experts without conclusive replication.
## UFOlogy and Investigative Work

### Major Documentaries on Anomalous Phenomena

Corbell's entry into documentary filmmaking focused on unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP) and related claims of government cover-ups, beginning with his 2018 production *Bob Lazar: Area 51 & Flying Saucers*. This film centers on Robert Lazar's assertions from 1989 that he was employed by a contractor at a site near Area 51 to reverse-engineer extraterrestrial spacecraft, including details on propulsion systems allegedly powered by an stable isotope of element 115, later synthesized as moscovium but without the properties Lazar described.  The documentary includes rare interviews with Lazar, who had largely avoided public appearances since his initial disclosures, alongside commentary from journalist George Knapp, who first broke Lazar's story. Corbell directed and produced the 97-minute feature, released on December 4, 2018, emphasizing Lazar's personal risks, including alleged threats and surveillance, though independent verification of Lazar's employment records or technical claims remains absent, with critics noting discrepancies in his educational credentials from MIT and Caltech.
In 2023, Corbell contributed to *TMZ Presents: UFO Revolution*, a three-part docuseries examining recent U.S. government disclosures on UAP, including Pentagon videos and whistleblower testimonies. The series highlights Corbell's own releases of military footage, such as the 2018 "Tic Tac" follow-up clips and a 2020 disc-shaped object over a military site, positioning them as evidence of non-human intelligence amid official acknowledgments of 144 unexplained cases in 2021 congressional reports. Corbell appears as an investigative figure advocating for transparency, interviewing sources like former intelligence officials and pushing for congressional hearings, though the production's ties to TMZ have drawn skepticism regarding sensationalism over empirical rigor. A second season, released January 2025, follows Corbell's efforts in Washington, D.C., including a new whistleblower reveal and scrutiny of UAP incidents like the "Jellyfish" object filmed in 2024, amid claims of institutional resistance to full disclosure.
These works underscore Corbell's approach of prioritizing primary footage and firsthand accounts over peer-reviewed analysis, with *Bob Lazar* achieving over 6,800 IMDb ratings averaging 5.6/10, reflecting polarized reception between proponents citing consistency in Lazar's narrative and detractors highlighting unproven elements like predicted Element 115's instability. *UFO Revolution* similarly amplifies declassified data, such as the 2021 Office of the Director of National Intelligence preliminary assessment, but lacks adjudication of anomalous versus prosaic explanations, aligning with Corbell's stated mission to catalyze public and official scrutiny despite limited corroborative physical evidence.
### Notable Footage Releases and Verifications

Corbell has released several videos captured by U.S. military personnel, often obtaining them through anonymous sources and conducting independent verifications before public disclosure. These include infrared and radar footage depicting unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP), with some authenticated by the Pentagon as genuine recordings but remaining unexplained in origin. His releases emphasize empirical visual data over speculation, though critics question sourcing chains and potential for misinterpretation.
In April 2021, Corbell released night-vision footage from the USS Russell off California in 2019, showing pyramid-shaped objects hovering above the ship. The Pentagon confirmed the video's authenticity as military-recorded material on April 16, 2021, stating it was under review by the UAP Task Force but offered no explanation for the objects' nature or propulsion. Corbell verified the footage through direct contact with naval personnel involved, asserting no evidence of drones or conventional aircraft matching the observed multi-layered structures.
Another release in May 2021 involved infrared video from the USS Omaha on July 15, 2019, capturing a spherical object transiting over the ocean before descending and submerging without splash, as observed by the crew. Corbell described the object's rapid maneuvers as inconsistent with known aerial vehicles, and the footage aligns with declassified Navy reports of similar incursions. The Pentagon has not disputed the video's provenance, though it classifies the event as unidentified rather than verified extraterrestrial. Radar data from the same incident, released concurrently, indicated up to 14 objects swarming the vessel over three days, corroborated by multiple Navy ships in the area.
In January 2024, Corbell shared thermographic footage from a U.S. forward operating base in Iraq in 2018, depicting a "jellyfish" UAP with appendages, traversing from sky to ground while evading detection. The Department of Defense acknowledged awareness of the video but has neither confirmed nor debunked it, prompting Corbell to argue its non-debunking implies preliminary authenticity pending further analysis. Independent reviews, including by aviation experts, note the object's thermal signature and trajectory defy prosaic explanations like balloons or birds due to sustained altitude and speed.
More recently, in July 2025, Corbell disclosed 2020 military footage of a disc-shaped UAP exhibiting controlled flight patterns, sourced from verified personnel. The video, captured via standard operational cameras, shows the object maintaining stability without visible propulsion, aligning with patterns in prior Navy encounters. No official verification has been issued by the Pentagon as of October 2025, though Corbell's track record with authenticated releases lends procedural credibility to the chain of custody.
Corbell's verification process typically involves cross-referencing metadata, consulting original operators, and ruling out hoaxes through forensic analysis, as detailed in his public statements. While these efforts have elevated discourse on UAP instrumentation, skeptics highlight the absence of peer-reviewed physical evidence tying footage to non-human intelligence, urging caution against overinterpretation of visual anomalies.
### Podcast and Collaborative Efforts

Corbell co-hosts the podcast *Weaponized* alongside investigative journalist George Knapp, launched in early 2023 as a weekly multi-platform series focused on unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAPs), government secrecy, and related unexplained events. The program features exclusive interviews with witnesses, declassified or leaked documents, and analyses of military footage, aiming to document patterns in UAP encounters and institutional responses. Episodes often include audio and video evidence obtained through whistleblowers or freedom of information requests, such as radar data of multiple objects interacting with naval vessels.
Through *Weaponized*, Corbell and Knapp have collaborated on verifying and publicizing specific UAP incidents, including a 2024 episode revealing military-filmed footage of anomalous objects, which they described as originating from classified sources. Their joint efforts extend to cross-referencing eyewitness accounts with technical data, such as in discussions of UAP incursions over military sites, emphasizing empirical patterns over speculative narratives. This partnership builds on Knapp's decades of reporting on Area 51 and UFO claims, with Corbell providing documentary-style sourcing and visual breakdowns.
Beyond the podcast, Corbell's collaborations include joint appearances at UFO conferences and media events with Knapp, such as the 2023 McMenamins UFO Festival, where they presented shared research on historical UAP cases. These efforts have facilitated the release of footage like the USS Omaha UAP video, co-analyzed for characteristics including rapid submersion and multi-object coordination, drawing from naval sensor data dated to 2019. Corbell has attributed the productivity of these collaborations to complementary skills: Knapp's journalistic access to insiders and Corbell's focus on archival verification.
## Controversies and Criticisms

### Accusations of Hype and Credibility Issues

Critics within the UFO research community and skeptical analysts have accused Jeremy Corbell of prioritizing sensationalism over empirical verification, often releasing ambiguous footage with dramatic narratives that amplify unconfirmed anomalous claims. For instance, in his 2018 documentary *Bob Lazar: Area 51 & Flying Saucers*, Corbell portrays Lazar's accounts of reverse-engineering alien craft at S-4 as credible despite longstanding disputes over Lazar's educational credentials from MIT and Caltech, which lack corroborating records, and his employment history at Los Alamos, contradicted by official personnel logs. These discrepancies, highlighted by investigators since Lazar's 1989 emergence, undermine the film's evidentiary foundation, with skeptics arguing Corbell's defense relies on anecdotal sincerity rather than testable data.
Corbell's pattern of publicizing thermal or low-resolution videos as potential extraterrestrial evidence has drawn scrutiny for lacking independent authentication, fostering perceptions of hype-driven disclosure. The January 2024 release of the "jellyfish" UAP video—allegedly from a 2018 infrared recording over a U.S. base in Iraq—depicts a shape with trailing appendages moving against wind, which Corbell described as a possible transmedium craft exhibiting non-ballistic motion. However, physicists and aviation experts have proposed prosaic explanations, such as a mylar balloon with attached debris or a bird in thermal bloom, citing the object's apparent lack of structural mass, absence of propulsion signatures, and behavioral inconsistencies with advanced technology under observed conditions.
Analyses of the broader UAP attention economy suggest Corbell's media ventures, including the *Weaponized* podcast co-hosted with George Knapp, contribute to "infotainment" dynamics where whistleblower testimonies are amplified for audience engagement, potentially eroding trust through unvetted escalation of claims. While Corbell maintains his releases advance transparency by bypassing institutional gatekeeping, detractors contend this approach risks conflating optical artifacts or misidentifications with paradigm-shifting evidence, as evidenced by post-release debunkings that revert many incidents to conventional explanations like lens flares, drones, or atmospheric phenomena. Such patterns have led some researchers to question whether his investigative style, rooted in rapid dissemination via platforms like TMZ and podcasts, prioritizes viral impact over causal scrutiny of provenance and physics.
### Specific Disputes and Empirical Scrutiny

Corbell's 2024 release of the "Jellyfish" UAP video, sourced from a military thermal imaging device and described by him as evidence of an unidentified object exhibiting anomalous flight characteristics during a base incursion, faced immediate empirical challenges. Independent analysis highlighted the footage's low resolution and lack of discernible propulsion or mass displacement, consistent with a lightweight balloon or small drone drifting in wind currents rather than advanced technology. The object's elongated, tentacle-like appearance in infrared was attributed to thermal blooming and atmospheric distortion effects, phenomena replicable in controlled tests with conventional objects, undermining claims of exotic origins without supporting telemetry data.
Similarly, the 2021 "pyramid" UAP footage from the USS Russell, authenticated by the Pentagon as genuine Navy material released by Corbell, depicted stacked triangular shapes near warships. The Pentagon included the incident in UAP examinations without providing an explanation. Skeptical analyses, such as by Mick West, propose the formations as bokeh effects from distant commercial aircraft lights captured through the camera's aperture in low-light conditions, consistent with flight data. Absent independent verification of anomalous kinematics, such as acceleration defying known physics, the video aligns with prosaic sensor limitations rather than empirical evidence for unidentified craft.
The 2023 "Mojave Triangle" video, presented by Corbell as capturing a silent triangular object witnessed by Marines at a training site, sparked disputes over chain-of-custody and interpretive bias. Scrutiny from aviation databases and eyewitness cross-examinations suggested misidentification of a stealth aircraft or drone in stealth mode, with the object's reported lack of noise attributable to high-altitude operation beyond audible range, rather than anti-gravity propulsion; no residual physical traces or multi-sensor data corroborated Corbell's extraordinary framing.
Pentagon reports, such as the 2024 All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office assessment, reinforce this scrutiny by finding no verifiable evidence of non-human intelligence across reviewed UAP cases, including those popularized by Corbell, emphasizing misperception over paradigm-shifting phenomena.

By June-July 2025, Corbell obtained and released disc-shaped UAP footage captured by U.S. military personnel, highlighting rapid maneuvers inconsistent with conventional aircraft. These releases, verified through source authentication processes Corbell detailed publicly, aimed to pressure declassification by demonstrating empirical anomalies in official archives.

At events like Contact in the Desert and RenderCon in 2025, he spoke on intersections of UAP evidence with artificial superintelligence, urging empirical prioritization over narrative control in disclosure debates. In 2025, Corbell published an open letter to the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO), criticizing its transparency and treatment of whistleblowers, demanding the full release of 2017 "Jellyfish" UAP footage from Lake Habbaniyah in Iraq—including the withheld segment depicting the object's entry and exit from the water—and calling for AARO's new director to denounce alleged slander against whistleblowers David Grusch and Matthew Brown. Corbell's efforts underscored a causal link between withheld footage and public distrust, advocating source-verified releases as the mechanism for advancing verifiable understanding of anomalous phenomena.
In March 2026, ahead of the April 3, 2026 release of *S4: The Bob Lazar Story*, Corbell posted a teaser on X stating "ACT natural?" accompanied by a link to the song "Go Home" by Angel Olsen from her 2022 album *Big Time*. The post functioned as a pun on "act natural," building anticipation for the documentary among followers, consistent with his style of cryptic promotions in UAP and UFO media.
This teaser relates to a new documentary revisiting [Bob Lazar](https://grokipedia.com/page/Bob_Lazar)'s claims, building on Corbell's earlier 2018 film *Bob Lazar: Area 51 & Flying Saucers*.
In late 2024, Corbell continued to influence UAP disclosure through direct contributions to Congress. He submitted a 12-page whistleblower report detailing the alleged "Immaculate Constellation" unacknowledged Special Access Program to journalist Michael Shellenberger, who referenced it during his testimony at the November 13, 2024, House Committee on Oversight and Accountability hearing titled "Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena: Exposing the Truth."
During the hearing, Rep. Nancy Mace presented the report but credited only Shellenberger, omitting Corbell's name and role in its submission. Corbell publicly criticized this as a deliberate omission—describing it as an "erasure" observed in real time—that could endanger whistleblowers and undermine transparency efforts.

His work, including the 2024 documentary series "UFO Revolution," has shaped public perception by documenting UAP sightings over military and nuclear sites, fostering debates on existential questions like non-human intelligence while eroding trust in institutions perceived as evasive.
