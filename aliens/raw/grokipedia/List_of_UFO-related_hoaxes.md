# List of UFO-related hoaxes

                                     UFO-related hoaxes encompass deliberate fabrications of sightings, photographs, physical traces, or encounters purporting to demonstrate unidentified flying objects or extraterrestrial visitation, subsequently exposed through perpetrator confessions, forensic analysis of materials, or replication via conventional means.
These deceptions, often motivated by personal notoriety, financial gain, or psychological factors such as suggestibility, have proliferated since the [19th century](https://grokipedia.com/page/19th_century), beginning with sensationalized press accounts like the 1835 [Great Moon Hoax](https://grokipedia.com/page/Great_Moon_Hoax)—falsely attributing lunar discoveries to astronomer [John Herschel](https://grokipedia.com/page/John_Herschel)—and extending to 20th-century cases such as George Adamski's 1950s saucer photographs, debunked as arrangements of chicken incubators and other earthly objects via detailed image scrutiny. More recent exemplars include the 1987 Gulf Breeze sightings, where models matching the alleged UFOs were recovered from the claimant's attic, and the 1995 Alien Autopsy film, admitted by its creator [Ray Santilli](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ray_Santilli) as a staged reconstruction lacking authentic provenance.
Such hoaxes undermine the credibility of broader unidentified anomalous phenomena reports, as empirical investigations— including U.S. government reviews—reveal no verifiable evidence of extraterrestrial origins despite thousands of claims, with most attributable to misidentifications, optical illusions, or intentional fraud facilitated by advancing technology like photo-editing apps. This pattern highlights the necessity of rigorous, evidence-based verification to distinguish prosaic explanations from potential anomalies, as uncritical acceptance perpetuates misinformation amid institutional tendencies toward sensationalism over causal analysis.
## Historical Airship and Mystery Aircraft Hoaxes

### Late 19th-Century Mystery Airship Wave

The [mystery airship](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mystery_airship) wave of 1896–1897 consisted of widespread reports across the [United States](https://grokipedia.com/page/United_States) of unidentified aerial craft resembling dirigibles or powered [airship](https://grokipedia.com/page/Airship)s, beginning in November 1896 on the West Coast and peaking in the Midwest by April 1897. Sightings described elongated objects with lights, sometimes crewed by human inventors or extraterrestrial pilots, but investigations revealed most as fabrications by journalists and locals to exploit public fascination with emerging [aviation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aviation) amid sensational newspaper competition known as [yellow journalism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Yellow_journalism). No verifiable [physical evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Physical_Evidence), such as wreckage or functional prototypes, emerged, and contemporary airship technology—limited to unpowered balloons—could not account for reported speeds exceeding 100 miles per hour or maneuverability.
Early reports in [California](https://grokipedia.com/page/California), starting November 17, 1896, near Sacramento, described a bright light moving erratically, sparking a flurry of claims including landings and passenger interviews, but local officials and astronomers attributed many to misidentified stars like [Venus](https://grokipedia.com/page/Venus) or hoaxes involving lanterns and kites. The wave intensified in 1897 across [Kansas](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kansas), [Illinois](https://grokipedia.com/page/Illinois), and [Texas](https://grokipedia.com/page/Texas), with stories of secret American inventors building advanced ships fueled by rumors of figures like Solomon Andrews or fictional crews; however, no such inventors produced evidence, and tales often contradicted each other in design and origin. Media outlets propagated unverified accounts daily, such as the April 1897 Dallas Morning News reports of near-nightly sightings, which escalated in grandeur without corroboration, fueling collective delusion rather than genuine phenomena.
Prominent hoaxes included the April 17, 1897, "crash" near [Aurora, Texas](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aurora,_Texas), where reporter S. E. Haydon claimed an [airship](https://grokipedia.com/page/Airship) exploded into a [windmill](https://grokipedia.com/page/Windmill), killing a diminutive pilot buried in the local cemetery; no debris, bodies, or metallic fragments were ever recovered, and 20th-century probes, including U.S. [Air Force](https://grokipedia.com/page/Air_force) inquiries, confirmed it as a fabricated tale to increase [newspaper](https://grokipedia.com/page/Newspaper) sales amid a lull in stories. Another fabrication was Kansas farmer Alexander Hamilton's April 19, 1897, account of an [airship](https://grokipedia.com/page/Airship) abducting his cow, which he later admitted stemmed from a local "Liars' Club" contest, yet it circulated widely before retractions. These incidents exemplify how telegraph operators and editors amplified hoaxes, with some, like [Milwaukee](https://grokipedia.com/page/Milwaukee) police in 1897, identifying kites or balloons as culprits in alleged sightings.
Historians attribute the wave's cessation by May 1897 to exhausted novelty and debunkings, including astronomical explanations for luminous sightings and admissions of journalistic invention, marking it as an early instance of mass media-driven UFO hysteria without empirical basis. The absence of patents, prototypes, or independent witnesses to claimed interactions underscores the [hoax](https://grokipedia.com/page/Hoax) nature, contrasting with later verifiable aviation milestones like the [Wright brothers](https://grokipedia.com/page/Wright_brothers)' 1903 flight.
## Mid-20th-Century Sighting and Contactee Hoaxes

### George Adamski's Claims and Fabrications

[George Adamski](https://grokipedia.com/page/George_Adamski) (1891–1965), a Polish-born American occultist and self-proclaimed philosopher, gained prominence in the early 1950s by claiming physical contact with extraterrestrial beings from [Venus](https://grokipedia.com/page/Venus), Mars, and Saturn. Operating a hamburger stand near Mount Palomar, [California](https://grokipedia.com/page/California), Adamski asserted that he began photographing unidentified flying objects in the late [1940s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1940s) using a telescope, capturing over 500 images by the end of 1952, of which he considered 12 to be of high quality. These photographs depicted bell-shaped scout craft and cigar-shaped mother ships, which he said were Venusian vessels observed over Palomar Gardens.
On November 20, 1952, [Adamski](https://grokipedia.com/page/Adamski) claimed his most famous encounter occurred in the desert near [Desert Center, California](https://grokipedia.com/page/Desert_Center,_California), where he met a [humanoid](https://grokipedia.com/page/Humanoid) Venusian named Orthon, described as having sandy hair, soft skin, and a peaceful demeanor. According to [Adamski](https://grokipedia.com/page/Adamski), communication occurred via [telepathy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Telepathy) and hand gestures, with Orthon warning of humanity's misuse of [atomic energy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Atomic_energy). He documented this in his 1953 book *Flying Saucers Have Landed*, co-authored with [Desmond Leslie](https://grokipedia.com/page/Desmond_Leslie), and expanded on subsequent alleged flights inside spacecraft in *Inside the Space Ships* (1955), detailing tours of the [Moon](https://grokipedia.com/page/Moon) and meetings with "Space Brothers." 
Fabrications in Adamski's evidence became evident through detailed scrutiny of his photographs. One iconic image, purportedly showing a landed Venusian scout craft taken on December 13, 1952, was identified as a chicken egg incubator (brooder) modified with lights and a [infrared](https://grokipedia.com/page/Infrared) bulb mimicking [landing gear](https://grokipedia.com/page/Landing_gear) and exhaust; microscopic analysis revealed reflections from studio floodlights and inconsistencies like fiber threads from model construction. German rocket engineer [Wernher von Braun](https://grokipedia.com/page/Wernher_von_Braun) associate Walther Riedel examined Adamski's photos in 1953 and concluded they were hoaxes, citing artificial artifacts visible under magnification. Ufologist James W. Moseley, in a 1957 *Saucer News* exposé, compiled witness discrepancies and physical evidence, deeming Adamski's claims an elaborate deception motivated by profit from [books](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_Books), lectures, and cult-like following.
Official investigations reinforced skepticism. In 1953, Project Blue Book head Edward J. Ruppelt visited Adamski incognito and found his demonstrations unconvincing, later dismissing the photos as crude fakes in line with astronomer J. Allen Hynek's assessments. Adamski's assertions of habitable Venus conflicted with emerging astronomical data on its extreme heat and toxic atmosphere, undermining claims of human-like inhabitants. Accomplices, including witness Alice Wells, provided inconsistent accounts, and Adamski's prior occult activities—such as founding the Royal Order of Tibet in the 1930s—suggested a pattern of mystical entrepreneurship rather than genuine disclosure. Despite international tours and endorsements from figures like Queen Juliana's advisors in 1959, no verifiable physical evidence or independent corroboration ever materialized, leading investigators to classify his narrative as a hoax exploiting post-World War II UFO enthusiasm.
### 1967 Fabricated Flying Saucer Incidents

The 1967 British [flying saucer](https://grokipedia.com/page/Flying_saucer) hoax involved the coordinated placement of six fabricated "spaceships" across [southern England](https://grokipedia.com/page/Southern_England) on September 4, 1967, orchestrated by apprentice engineers at the Royal Aircraft Establishment in Farnborough to promote a student charity fundraising event known as Rag Week. The perpetrators, led by 22-year-old Chris Southall, constructed the saucers from [fiberglass](https://grokipedia.com/page/Fiberglass) shells coated in aluminum gel for a metallic appearance, each approximately 3 feet in diameter and equipped with internal mechanisms including a battery-powered [loudspeaker](https://grokipedia.com/page/Loudspeaker) emitting a humming sound activated by a mercury tilt switch, along with a [mixture](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mixture) of [flour](https://grokipedia.com/page/Flour) and [water](https://grokipedia.com/page/Water) intended to mimic alien residue upon inspection.
The devices were deployed under cover of darkness around 2 a.m. in a straight-line pattern spanning roughly 150 miles, at sites including [Clevedon](https://grokipedia.com/page/Clevedon) in [Somerset](https://grokipedia.com/page/Somerset), [Chippenham](https://grokipedia.com/page/Chippenham) in [Wiltshire](https://grokipedia.com/page/Wiltshire), Welford in [Berkshire](https://grokipedia.com/page/Berkshire), Winkfield near Ascot in [Berkshire](https://grokipedia.com/page/Berkshire), Sundridge Park Golf Club in Bromley (south [London](https://grokipedia.com/page/South_London)), and Rushenden on the Isle of Sheppey in [Kent](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kent). Discovered by locals that morning, the saucers prompted immediate emergency responses: police cordoned off areas treating them as potential alien craft or explosive devices, [bomb disposal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Bomb_disposal) teams were dispatched, the Royal [Air Force](https://grokipedia.com/page/Air_force) used a [helicopter](https://grokipedia.com/page/Helicopter) to [airlift](https://grokipedia.com/page/Airlift) one from Sheppey for examination, and the [Ministry of Defence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ministry_of_defence) initiated investigations amid public reports of fear and excitement. Media outlets, including the *Daily Mirror* and *New York Times*, amplified the story with headlines suggesting an extraterrestrial incursion, contributing to a spike in UFO sightings that year, with [UK](https://grokipedia.com/page/United_Kingdom) reports totaling 362.
The hoax was revealed later that evening when journalists confronted the students, who admitted responsibility to publicize their event and raise approximately £2,000 for charity, with no legal prosecutions despite initial threats of charges for wasting police time. Participants later reflected that the prank exploited heightened Cold War-era anxieties about unidentified aerial phenomena, though official responses were criticized for lacking coordinated UFO protocols, as evidenced by ad-hoc military involvement without a unified [government](https://grokipedia.com/page/Government) plan. This incident stands as one of the most elaborate UFO fabrications of the era, demonstrating how low-cost [engineering](https://grokipedia.com/page/Engineering) could simulate extraterrestrial landings and trigger widespread institutional reactions.
## Crashed UFO and Physical Evidence Hoaxes

### Alien Autopsy Footage Fabrication

The Alien Autopsy footage, released in 1995 by British music promoter [Ray Santilli](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ray_Santilli), purported to depict the dissection of a recovered extraterrestrial body from the 1947 [Roswell incident](https://grokipedia.com/page/Roswell_incident). Santilli claimed the 17-minute black-and-white film originated from U.S. military archives, obtained during a 1993 search for [Elvis Presley](https://grokipedia.com/page/Elvis_Presley) concert footage in the U.S., where a cameraman allegedly sold him degraded 1947 reels showing the procedure. The video featured masked figures in a sterile room using period-appropriate tools to examine a humanoid figure with an oversized head, six fingers, and no navel, removing organs amid discussions of biological anomalies.
The footage premiered at a press conference in [London](https://grokipedia.com/page/London) on May 4, 1995, before airing globally, including on [Fox](https://grokipedia.com/page/Fox) Television's *Alien Autopsy: Fact or Fiction?* special on August 28, 1995, which drew 20 million U.S. viewers despite immediate [skepticism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Skepticism) from experts noting anatomical errors like mismatched shadows, incorrect surgical techniques, and visible seams on the "body." Analyses by forensic pathologist John Altshuler and [special effects](https://grokipedia.com/page/Special_effect) experts highlighted inconsistencies, such as the entity's flexible limbs contradicting [rigor mortis](https://grokipedia.com/page/Rigor_mortis) and tools anachronistic for 1947. Santilli earned millions from licensing deals, but [Fox](https://grokipedia.com/page/Fox) later labeled it a [hoax](https://grokipedia.com/page/Hoax) after investigations revealed no corroborating [evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Evidence) from Roswell witnesses or military records.
In a 2006 documentary, *Alien Autopsy: The True Story*, Santilli confessed to fabricating the footage as a "reconstruction" after the supposed original reels deteriorated beyond usability, using a dummy constructed from sheep brains, chicken intestines, and animal parts, filmed by effects artist John Humphreys in a London studio with actors and prosthetics. He maintained witnessing authentic [1947](https://grokipedia.com/page/1947) material during his U.S. trip, which informed the recreation, though no physical evidence of this original has surfaced, and Humphreys detailed sourcing materials from a [butcher](https://grokipedia.com/page/Butcher) for the viscera. Skeptics, including ufologist [Stanton Friedman](https://grokipedia.com/page/Friedman), dismissed Santilli's partial-authenticity claim as unsubstantiated, attributing the hoax's success to exploiting Roswell lore amid [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s) UFO revival.
The fabrication influenced pop culture, inspiring films like *Alien Autopsy* (2006), but forensic reviews confirmed no extraterrestrial elements, with the "alien" exhibiting human-like bone structure and fabricated fluids inconsistent with real autopsies. Despite the admission, some enthusiasts persist in viewing it as partially genuine, though peer-reviewed dismissals and lack of verifiable provenance affirm its status as a deliberate [deception](https://grokipedia.com/page/Deception) for profit.
### Berwyn Mountain "UFO Crash" Hoax

On the evening of January 23, 1974, residents near the Berwyn Mountains in [north Wales](https://grokipedia.com/page/North_Wales), specifically around Llandrillo in [Merionethshire](https://grokipedia.com/page/Merionethshire), reported a series of unusual phenomena including a loud explosion, ground tremors, and bright lights descending in the sky. Local nurse Pat Evans, responding to calls about a possible [aircraft](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aircraft) crash, drove toward the site and claimed to observe an elongated object with a red glow on the hillside, along with smaller lights moving nearby, interpreting it as a downed UFO potentially containing deceased occupants. These accounts fueled immediate speculation of an extraterrestrial craft impact, drawing comparisons to the 1947 [Roswell incident](https://grokipedia.com/page/Roswell_incident) and prompting UFO enthusiasts to label it the "Welsh Roswell."
Ministry of Defence (MoD) files, declassified and released by the National Archives in 2010, detail an official investigation that attributed the events to coincidental natural occurrences rather than any anomalous craft. Seismic records confirmed a magnitude 3.5 earthquake epicentered approximately 10 miles southwest of Llandrillo at 9:28 p.m., causing the reported shaking and bang, while multiple witnesses across the UK described a bright fireball meteor streaking from northwest to southeast around 10 p.m., responsible for the lights.  The MoD noted five contemporaneous UFO sightings elsewhere in Britain, consistent with the meteor's visibility, and found no evidence of a crash site, wreckage, or military retrieval operation despite claims of secretive activity. Reports of lights on the ground were likely misperceptions of the meteor's trail or rare earthquake-induced luminous phenomena, for which empirical precedents exist but no extraterrestrial link.
Subsequent scrutiny has reinforced the prosaic explanation, with no verifiable [physical evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Physical_Evidence)—such as [debris](https://grokipedia.com/page/Debris) or [radiation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Radiation) anomalies—emerging to support crash claims, and [witness](https://grokipedia.com/page/Witness) testimonies varying widely without corroboration. Proponents like investigator Russell Kellett have alleged a government cover-up involving suppressed seismic data or fabricated natural attributions, but these assertions rely on anecdotal inconsistencies rather than empirical falsification of the [earthquake](https://grokipedia.com/page/Earthquake) and [meteor](https://grokipedia.com/page/METEOR) records. The incident's persistence as a UFO [narrative](https://grokipedia.com/page/Narrative) exemplifies how misidentified terrestrial events, amplified by initial [ambiguity](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ambiguity) and media [sensationalism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sensationalism), evolve into unsubstantiated hoax-like lore absent causal evidence for extraterrestrial involvement.
## Photographic and Pre-Digital Visual Hoaxes

### Billy Meier Photograph and Artifact Forgeries

Eduard "Billy" Meier, a Swiss farmer born February 3, 1937, claimed physical evidence of extraterrestrial contacts through photographs of alleged "beamships" from Plejaran visitors, taken primarily between January 1975 and October 1980 near his Schmidrüti property. These images, numbering over 1,000, depicted metallic disc- and dome-shaped [craft](https://grokipedia.com/page/Craft) against Swiss countryside backdrops, purportedly captured spontaneously during contacts with beings like Semjase. Meier asserted the craft were too advanced for human replication, dismissing hoax accusations as [disinformation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Disinformation) by skeptics or authorities.
Photographic analyses, however, identified fabrication techniques consistent with small-scale models. Enlargements revealed suspension strings, disproportionate shadows, and lack of atmospheric distortion expected from distant objects, indicating close-range props rather than large craft. Background elements matched Meier's immediate surroundings, with [parallax](https://grokipedia.com/page/Parallax) shifts absent in sequential shots, suggesting static setups rather than motion. Independent investigators replicated identical images using miniature models—constructed from aluminum foil, plastic, and household items—suspended by [fishing line](https://grokipedia.com/page/Fishing_line) or wire against the same rural vistas, achieving exact visual matches under similar lighting conditions.
Meier's ex-wife, Kalliope Zafiris Meier, provided [sworn testimony](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sworn_testimony) in the late [1970s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1970s) and [1980s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1980s) affirming the use of such models, stating she assisted in fabricating them from scrap materials like yogurt lids and coat hangers, then photographing the setups to simulate UFOs. She described Meier's motive as generating income and attention for his claims, corroborated by inconsistencies in his accounts of photo origins. No unaltered originals or witness-verified negatives have surfaced to counter these findings, and digital enhancements of prints further exposed editing artifacts and model seams.
Accompanying artifacts included metal samples Meier claimed were fragments from beamship exhaust or repairs, presented from 1976 onward with assertions of impossible terrestrial manufacture. Laboratory tests by proponents like [Marcel Vogel](https://grokipedia.com/page/Marcel_Vogel) reported anomalous crystalline structures and self-healing properties, but subsequent independent analyses classified them as conventional aluminum alloys (e.g., 6061 series) with [titanium dioxide](https://grokipedia.com/page/Titanium_dioxide) coatings and etched patterns producible via acid immersion or [anodizing](https://grokipedia.com/page/Anodizing) processes available since the mid-20th century. Replications by skeptical groups matched the samples' microstructures and isotopic signatures without exotic elements, undermining claims of extraterrestrial origin. No peer-reviewed studies validate the samples' uniqueness, and chain-of-custody issues—samples handled solely by Meier's associates—preclude objective verification.
### Ilkley Moor "Alien" Photograph Hoax

On December 1, 1987, Philip Spencer—a pseudonym for a retired [Metropolitan Police](https://grokipedia.com/page/Metropolitan_Police) officer who had relocated to [West Yorkshire](https://grokipedia.com/page/West_Yorkshire)—claimed to have encountered and photographed a small humanoid entity while walking across [Ilkley Moor](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ilkley_Moor) near the town of [Ilkley](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ilkley) in [northern England](https://grokipedia.com/page/Northern_England). Spencer stated that around 8:15 a.m., en route to visit his sister in a nearby town, his pocket [compass](https://grokipedia.com/page/Compass) began spinning uncontrollably, indicating reversed polarity. He then observed a 4-foot-tall, green-skinned figure with elongated limbs and a domed head, which appeared to gesture toward a hovering, saucer-shaped [craft](https://grokipedia.com/page/Craft) emitting a beam of light. Interpreting the entity as non-human and potentially threatening, Spencer raised his camera—a standard 35mm model loaded with black-and-white film—and captured a single image of the figure as it moved away from him toward the UFO before vanishing. He subsequently experienced about 90 minutes of unaccounted-for time, arriving at his destination around 10:00 a.m. with no clear memory of the interval.
The developed photograph showed a blurry, shadowy silhouette of a short, featureless being against a hazy, light-emitting backdrop, which Spencer and UFO proponents described as evidence of an extraterrestrial. The image measured approximately 1.2 meters in apparent height within the frame, with no discernible facial features or fine details due to graininess and poor focus. Spencer reported physical effects post-incident, including disorientation and a lingering sense of unease, prompting him to contact local UFO investigators. On March 16, 1988, he underwent hypnotic regression therapy administered by clinical [psychologist](https://grokipedia.com/page/Psychologist) Dr. Jim Singleton, during which he recounted fragmented memories of abduction: being floated aboard the craft, undergoing non-invasive medical scans, and receiving telepathic warnings from the entities about humanity's [environmental degradation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Environmental_degradation) and nuclear risks. These details, however, emerged solely under [hypnosis](https://grokipedia.com/page/Hypnosis), a method known to facilitate false memories through suggestion and reconstruction rather than reliable retrieval.
Initial investigations by British UFO researchers Jenny Randles and Peter Hough examined the original negative, which [Kodak](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kodak) laboratories authenticated as undeveloped and untampered, showing no double exposure or chemical manipulation—though the film's low quality precluded digital enhancement or forensic clarity at the time. Optician and photo analyst [Dr. Bruce Maccabee](https://grokipedia.com/page/Bruce_Maccabee) later reviewed prints, estimating the figure's distance at around 40-50 meters based on foreground rocks, but could not rule out prosaic explanations like a costumed person or propped object exploiting the moor's fog-prone visibility. The [compass](https://grokipedia.com/page/compass) anomaly, while reported consistently, lacks independent verification and could stem from localized geomagnetic interference common in moorland terrain or intentional pre-testing by the witness. No traces of the alleged craft's landing site, radiation, or biological residue were documented despite searches.
Classified as a hoax by skeptics due to the absence of corroborative [evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Evidence), the incident exemplifies UFO claims reliant on ambiguous visuals and unverifiable [testimony](https://grokipedia.com/page/Testimony). The photograph's vagueness permits multiple interpretations—a misidentified hiker in green attire, a discarded dummy, or even [pareidolia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Pareidolia) in low-light conditions—without favoring an extraterrestrial [hypothesis](https://grokipedia.com/page/Hypothesis), which demands extraordinary substantiation unmet here. Spencer's police background lent initial credibility in UFO lore, yet his adoption of a [pseudonym](https://grokipedia.com/page/Pseudonym) and pursuit of [hypnosis](https://grokipedia.com/page/Hypnosis) suggest motive for narrative embellishment amid 1980s abduction mania. Mainstream scientific scrutiny, prioritizing empirical [falsifiability](https://grokipedia.com/page/Falsifiability), dismisses the case as unproven [folklore](https://grokipedia.com/page/Folklore), with hypnotic elements particularly suspect given studies showing such sessions amplify [confabulation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Confabulation) over factual recall. No peer-reviewed analysis has validated the entity as non-human, aligning the event with documented patterns of fabricated or psychologically induced UFO encounters.
## Modern Digital, Video, and Artifact Hoaxes

### CGI and Drone-Based Video Fabrications

CGI and drone-based video fabrications represent a modern evolution in UFO hoaxes, leveraging accessible digital tools to create convincing simulations of unidentified aerial phenomena. With the proliferation of consumer-grade software for [visual effects](https://grokipedia.com/page/Visual_effects) and affordable drone technology, individuals have produced videos that mimic erratic flight patterns, luminous orbs, or structured craft, often disseminated via [social media](https://grokipedia.com/page/Social_media) for attention or profit. These fabrications exploit public fascination with UFOs, particularly amid renewed government disclosures on unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP), but forensic analysis by experts frequently reveals artifacts like inconsistent lighting, unnatural motion blur, or metadata discrepancies indicative of [post-production](https://grokipedia.com/page/Post-production) manipulation.
A prominent CGI hoax occurred in February 2013 when a video surfaced showing a disc-shaped UFO descending and landing near a parked car on a foggy rural road, accompanied by mechanical sounds and a reacting witness. The footage, which garnered millions of views, was created entirely by Greek-Canadian VFX artist Konstantinos Tsirbas using Autodesk Maya and Nuke software; he later confirmed it as a demonstration of photorealistic CGI capabilities, noting that the car's reflections, environmental interactions, and camera shake were all digitally fabricated to appear authentic. Tsirbas emphasized the intent was to highlight how advanced rendering could deceive viewers, with the video's viral spread underscoring vulnerabilities in visual verification.
The 2011 Jerusalem UFO videos, uploaded in late January, depicted a glowing orb hovering above the [Dome of the Rock](https://grokipedia.com/page/Dome_of_the_Rock) before ascending rapidly, captured from multiple angles by purported witnesses. Investigations identified hoax elements, including synchronized audio exclamations added after filming—evident from mismatched lip-sync and ambient noise—and one "third angle" proven to be a digital fabrication derived from a static tourist [photograph](https://grokipedia.com/page/Photograph), with motion artificially imposed via editing software. Skeptics and media outlets, citing violations of physics like impossible acceleration without sonic booms, concluded the event was staged, possibly using a model or light source, though digital enhancement amplified its realism; no physical evidence or independent corroboration emerged despite the site's high visibility.
More recent fabrications incorporate AI-generated content, such as a December 2024 video claiming UFOs over [Maryland](https://grokipedia.com/page/Maryland), featuring three orbs maneuvering near power lines. [Reuters](https://grokipedia.com/page/Reuters) fact-checkers traced it to AI tools like [Midjourney](https://grokipedia.com/page/Midjourney), with hallmarks including synthetic textures and improbable light refraction patterns inconsistent with real footage; the creator shared similar generated clips on social platforms, admitting experimental intent rather than deception, yet it fueled online speculation. Similarly, a 2023 clip of a UFO impacting the moon's surface was identified as CGI from a 3D artist's portfolio, displaying uniform particle simulations absent in authentic astronomical recordings.
Drone-based hoaxes, while less frequently admitted, involve deploying unmanned aerial vehicles with LED lights or attachments to replicate UFO maneuvers like hovering or sudden direction changes, capitalizing on drones' quiet operation and [remote control](https://grokipedia.com/page/Remote_control). Though specific confessions are rare, analyses of viral videos often reveal drone signatures, such as FAA-compliant altitude limits or propeller noise spectra matching commercial models like [DJI](https://grokipedia.com/page/DJI) models, distinguishing them from genuine anomalies; intentional staging has been inferred in cases where operators violate no-fly zones near crowds to provoke reactions, blending mundane [technology](https://grokipedia.com/page/Technology) with extraterrestrial claims for virality.
### Jaime Maussan-Promoted "Alien Mummy" Hoaxes

Jaime Maussan, a Mexican journalist and ufologist, has repeatedly promoted mummified remains as evidence of extraterrestrial beings, with multiple instances later determined to be fabrications involving assembled bones and synthetic materials.  These efforts, often tied to specimens from Peru's Nazca region, have fueled controversies since at least 2017, when Maussan first showcased three-fingered "alien" mummies during public presentations, claiming they exhibited non-human anatomical features such as elongated skulls and unusual hand structures. Independent analyses, including radiographic imaging, revealed inconsistencies like mismatched bone densities and evidence of manual assembly, contradicting claims of ancient extraterrestrial origins.
In September 2023, Maussan escalated his advocacy by presenting two small, desiccated "non-human" bodies to Mexico's Congress during a UFO hearing, asserting under oath that they were recovered from diatom mines near Peru's Nazca Lines, dated to approximately 1,000 years old via carbon-14 testing, and contained 30% unidentified DNA not matching known terrestrial species.  The specimens, measuring about 2-3 feet in length with three-fingered hands and metallic implants, were displayed in glass cases alongside X-rays purportedly showing egg-like structures and fetal remains inside one. Maussan collaborated with purported scientists who claimed the bodies represented a new species, emphasizing their tridactyl limbs and absence of human dental or skeletal norms.
Scientific scrutiny quickly exposed these as hoaxes. Forensic examinations by Peruvian experts in January 2024 of similar "alien dolls" seized at Lima's airport confirmed construction from animal bones (e.g., birds and dogs), [human](https://grokipedia.com/page/Human) fetal remains in some cases, and modern synthetic glues like [epoxy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Epoxy), with no evidence of antiquity or extraterrestrial biology. DNA sequencing on the 2023 specimens yielded predominantly [human](https://grokipedia.com/page/Human) genetic material, with anomalies attributable to degradation or [contamination](https://grokipedia.com/page/Contamination) rather than alien origins, as affirmed by multiple independent labs rejecting Maussan's selective test interpretations. Anthropologists noted the three-fingered design echoed cultural artifacts from pre-Columbian [Peru](https://grokipedia.com/page/Peru) but lacked verifiable provenance, linking the proliferation to illicit grave robbing incentivized by "alien" speculation in Nazca's desiccating salt flats.
Maussan's history includes prior debunked UFO claims, such as fabricated videos and artifacts from the [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s), undermining his credibility among skeptics who argue his promotions prioritize [sensationalism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sensationalism) over empirical validation. Despite dismissals from bodies like NASA's UFO study panel, which found no substantiation for [non-human](https://grokipedia.com/page/Non-human) intelligence in such remains, Maussan has continued defending the mummies, threatening legal action against critics while insisting on further undisclosed tests. These episodes exemplify how unverified physical "evidence" can propagate [misinformation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Misinformation), prompting calls for rigorous peer-reviewed analysis before public endorsement.
### 2016 Photoshopped "Ancient Egyptian Alien Coin" Hoax

No authentic coin found in Egypt depicts an extraterrestrial. Claims of such a discovery are based on a 2016 hoax involving digitally altered (photoshopped) images of a genuine Roman medallion from the Cabinet de Médailles in Paris. The original medallion features portraits related to Emperor Hadrian's companion Antinous and the god Dionysus, but the hoax modified one side to resemble a "Grey alien" figure associated with UFO lore. No credible archaeological or numismatic evidence supports the existence of ancient Egyptian coins showing extraterrestrials.
