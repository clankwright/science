# List of UFO organizations

                                     A list of UFO organizations compiles entities dedicated to the investigation, documentation, and analysis of unidentified flying objects (UFOs), rebranded in recent official contexts as unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP), which involve reports of aerial observations defying conventional explanations. These groups emerged prominently after the 1947 [Kenneth Arnold](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kenneth_Arnold) sighting and subsequent [Roswell incident](https://grokipedia.com/page/Roswell_incident), prompting both government inquiries and civilian initiatives to catalog sightings, interview witnesses, and pursue potential scientific insights into anomalous events.
Such organizations span government programs, like the U.S. Air Force's [Project Blue Book](https://grokipedia.com/page/Project_Blue_Book) (1947–1969), which examined over 12,000 reports and concluded that the vast majority stemmed from misidentifications of stars, aircraft, or atmospheric effects, and private nonprofits such as the [Mutual UFO Network](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mutual_UFO_Network) (MUFON), established in 1969 to conduct field investigations and maintain databases of global encounters. International counterparts include Russia's [Kosmopoisk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kosmopoisk) and China's UFO Research Organization, reflecting varied national approaches to probing these phenomena amid cultural and geopolitical influences.
While these bodies have amassed extensive anecdotal data and advanced methodologies for sighting verification, comprehensive reviews, including the Pentagon's [All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office](https://grokipedia.com/page/All-domain_Anomaly_Resolution_Office) (AARO) historical report, affirm no [empirical evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Empirical_evidence) exists for extraterrestrial technology or reverse-engineered alien craft, attributing unresolved cases to [sensor](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sensor) artifacts, classified programs, or insufficient data rather than otherworldly origins. Controversies persist, with critics decrying many civilian groups' emphasis on extraterrestrial hypotheses over prosaic alternatives, yet their archival efforts preserve primary accounts for future scrutiny.
## International Organizations

### Active International Organizations

The [Mutual UFO Network](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mutual_UFO_Network) (MUFON), founded in 1969, operates as a [nonprofit organization](https://grokipedia.com/page/Nonprofit_organization) with civilian volunteers investigating UFO sightings worldwide, maintaining field investigators in over 50 countries and processing thousands of reports annually through its database. It conducts field investigations, hosts symposia, and publishes findings, emphasizing empirical data collection despite criticisms of inconsistent methodologies in some cases.
The Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies (SCU), established in 2019, comprises scientists, engineers, and professionals from diverse nations focused on rigorous, evidence-based analysis of UAP, rejecting unsubstantiated claims in favor of peer-reviewed studies and instrumentation. The group has organized annual conferences, including one scheduled for June 6–8, 2025, in [Huntsville, Alabama](https://grokipedia.com/page/Huntsville,_Alabama), and contributes technical papers on topics like [radar](https://grokipedia.com/page/Radar) data and optical analyses of anomalous events. Its membership includes experts from academia and industry, promoting transparency through public datasets while acknowledging the scarcity of verifiable high-quality evidence for extraterrestrial origins.
The Extraterrestrial and UFO Research Organisation, International ([EURO](https://grokipedia.com/page/Euro)), an India-based nonprofit, engages civilian volunteers in studying UFO reports and ancient astronaut theories, with ongoing activities documented through social media updates as of 2023. It emphasizes [cross-cultural](https://grokipedia.com/page/Cross-cultural) historical correlations but lacks peer-reviewed publications, relying instead on volunteer testimonies and archival research.
### Defunct International Organizations

Contact International, founded in 1967 in the United Kingdom by [Brinsley Le Poer Trench](https://grokipedia.com/page/Earl_of_Clancarty) ([Earl of Clancarty](https://grokipedia.com/page/Earl_of_Clancarty)), operated as an international network for UFO research and [contactee](https://grokipedia.com/page/Contactee) experiences. The organization published the quarterly *Awareness* journal, which documented UFO sightings and individual accounts, alongside the biannual *UFO Register* compiling corroborated reports. By the 1980s, it maintained members across more than 30 countries, fostering cross-border exchange of ufological data. [Trench](https://grokipedia.com/page/Trench), who spearheaded the first UFO debate in the UK [House of Lords](https://grokipedia.com/page/House_of_Lords) on January 18, 1979, led the group until its cessation as a former entity following his death.
The International Flying Saucer Bureau (IFSB), initiated in 1952 by [Albert K. Bender](https://grokipedia.com/page/Albert_K._Bender) in the United States, aimed to unite UFO enthusiasts worldwide through coordinated investigations. It distributed the *Space Review* newsletter to international subscribers and launched [World Contact Day](https://grokipedia.com/page/World_Contact_Day) on March 15, 1953, calling for collective telepathic outreach to potential extraterrestrial intelligences. The bureau rapidly expanded but dissolved in late 1953 when Bender halted activities, attributing the closure to threats from unidentified figures opposed to UFO inquiries.
## European Organizations

### Belarusian Organizations

The Belarusian Ufological Committee (UFOCOM), also known as the Ufological Community, is a non-commercial, informal public organization focused on investigating unidentified flying objects (UFOs) and anomalous phenomena in [Belarus](https://grokipedia.com/page/Belarus) using a scientific methodology emphasizing data accumulation and analysis. Founded on May 1, 2001, in [Gomel](https://grokipedia.com/page/Gomel) by a group of enthusiasts, it operates without formal legal registration and comprises approximately 20 active members across regional branches in cities including [Minsk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Minsk), [Gomel](https://grokipedia.com/page/Gomel), and [Vitebsk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Vitebsk), with a total network of around 50 participants. 
UFOCOM conducts annual field expeditions to document UFO sightings, poltergeist activity, astroarchaeological sites, and meteoritic events, often collaborating internationally with groups like the Russian [Kosmopoisk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kosmopoisk) project since 2005. Notable activities include cataloging local observations, such as a cigar-shaped luminous object reported over [Vitebsk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Vitebsk) in February 1992, and studies of [paranormal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Paranormal) [folklore](https://grokipedia.com/page/Folklore) like cemetery apparitions tied to Belarusian traditions.  The group publishes reports, maintains an online database of cases, and has issued volumes like *Таинственная Беларусь* exploring mythological and anomalous elements in Belarusian history. Coordinated by [Ilya](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ilya) Butov since 2005, UFOCOM remains active, receiving hundreds of annual reports from witnesses while prioritizing empirical verification over unsubstantiated claims.  No other major independent UFO organizations have been documented in Belarus, with related efforts often overlapping through UFOCOM's network or informal [Kosmopoisk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kosmopoisk) affiliates.
### Estonian Organizations

EUFORA (Estonian UFO Research Association), registered as a non-profit association (MTÜ) on September 12, 2023, investigates UFO phenomena through scientific methods, including the collection and analysis of sighting reports via its associated UFO register. The group maintains an online database documenting environmental anomalies and UFO cases, such as light objects observed in Muuga on August 15, 2025, and mobile light phenomena in various Estonian locations from 2024 to 2025, with at least six recent entries cataloged under unique identifiers like UR-609 to UR-614. EUFORA collaborates with international researchers and emphasizes empirical documentation over speculative interpretations.
Preceding EUFORA, Estonian ufology was dominated by networks founded by Igor Volke (1950–2024), who began systematic UFO and anomaly investigations in 1972 and amassed approximately 800 cases over five decades. Volke established AKRAK (Anomaalsete Keskkonnanähtuste Uurimisgrupp), an early group focused on anomalous environmental phenomena including UFOs, in 1985; it became inactive by the early 2000s. AKRAK was succeeded by EUFON (Estonian UFO Network), formed around 2005 as a loose coordination of researchers, though it operated largely informally and in relative obscurity by 2020, with Volke handling about 12 UFO reports annually and sharing cases internationally.
Following Volke's death on November 14, 2024, the Igor Volke Anomalistika Keskus was established posthumously to preserve his archives and continue research into UFOs alongside broader anomalies like energy fields and crop circles, involving collaborators such as Erkki Ergma and Lauri Hermann. This center builds on Volke's publications, including *Ufopäevikud* (1998) and *Täiendatud ufopäevikud* (2017), which compile global and Estonian UFO reports from the 1940s onward with emphasis on verifiable witness accounts. Estonian UFO efforts remain niche, prioritizing data collection over institutional advocacy, with no large-scale government involvement documented.
### French Organizations

The French approach to unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP), historically termed unidentified flying objects (UFOs), features a unique government-sanctioned entity alongside smaller civilian groups. Unlike many nations, [France](https://grokipedia.com/page/France) established an official investigative body in 1977 amid a wave of sightings, reflecting institutional acknowledgment of the need for systematic [data collection](https://grokipedia.com/page/Data_collection) and analysis rather than dismissal. This contrasts with predominantly private efforts elsewhere, though civilian associations persist, often focusing on regional investigations or advocacy for extraterrestrial hypotheses.
GEIPAN (Groupe d'Études et d'Informations sur les Phénomènes Aérospatiaux Non-identifiés), operational since 2005 under the French space agency [CNES](https://grokipedia.com/page/CNES), serves as the primary public-facing organization for UAP studies. It succeeded GEPAN (1977–1988), which handled initial official inquiries into over 1,000 cases, and SEPRA (1999–2004), amid efforts to centralize reporting. GEIPAN maintains a database of approximately 8,000 reports as of 2023, classifying them into categories like identified (e.g., atmospheric phenomena or aircraft) or unexplained (about 5% of cases, lacking sufficient data for resolution). The unit employs four full-time staff and volunteers for fieldwork, emphasizing empirical verification over speculative origins, with public access to declassified files since 2007.
Civilian efforts include historical groups like the Comité Nord-Est des Groupes Ufologiques (CNEGU), formed in 1978 as a loose network of regional ufologists studying sightings and archives, though its activities waned over time. Smaller associations, such as [France](https://grokipedia.com/page/France) Ufologie (established [1992](https://grokipedia.com/page/1992)), focus on data compilation and public education, but lack the resources or institutional backing of [GEIPAN](https://grokipedia.com/page/GEIPAN), often relying on volunteer reports prone to anecdotal [bias](https://grokipedia.com/page/Bias). These groups occasionally critique official findings for underemphasizing anomalous cases, yet empirical reviews show most civilian claims align with prosaic explanations upon [scrutiny](https://grokipedia.com/page/Scrutiny).
### Swedish Organizations

UFO-Sverige, formally known as Riksorganisationen UFO-Sverige, is Sweden's principal science-oriented organization dedicated to the investigation of unidentified flying objects and aerial phenomena. Founded during [Easter](https://grokipedia.com/page/Easter) weekend in 1970, it functions as a non-profit entity that systematically collects public reports—approximately 250 per year—and subjects them to empirical analysis, attributing over 90% to identifiable causes such as atmospheric effects, [aircraft](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aircraft), or misperceptions. The group maintains a national reporting center and conducts field investigations, emphasizing methodological rigor over speculative interpretations.
The Archives for the Unexplained (AFU), originally established in 1973 as the Archives for UFO Research, operates as a foundation preserving extensive documentation on UFO sightings, ufological studies, forteana, [cryptozoology](https://grokipedia.com/page/Cryptozoology), and related anomalous phenomena. Located in [Norrköping](https://grokipedia.com/page/Norrk%C3%B6ping), its collections—spanning 4.2 kilometers of shelving as of 2024—include reports, clippings, books, and artifacts from global sources, with particular depth on Swedish cases and the activities of approximately 150 historical UFO groups in the country. Unlike investigative bodies, AFU prioritizes archival preservation over active case resolution, serving researchers by cataloging materials that document patterns in eyewitness accounts and physical traces without endorsing extraterrestrial hypotheses.
Historical records indicate a proliferation of smaller, often short-lived UFO societies in [Sweden](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sweden) since the mid-20th century, many of which contributed materials to AFU but ceased operations amid declining public interest or internal challenges. Prominent among defunct entities were early missionary-oriented groups that evolved into or merged with UFO-Sverige, reflecting a shift toward evidence-based [inquiry](https://grokipedia.com/page/Inquiry).
### Russian Organizations

Kosmopoisk, established in 1980, operates as a key non-governmental entity in [Russia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Russia) focused on [ufology](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ufology), [cryptozoology](https://grokipedia.com/page/Cryptozoology), and investigations of anomalous atmospheric and terrestrial phenomena. The group conducts annual expeditions to reported UFO sighting locations, collects eyewitness testimonies, and examines physical traces such as alleged "angel hair" residues or crop formations. In July 2015, Kosmopoisk activists documented intricate crop circles near [Krasnodar](https://grokipedia.com/page/Krasnodar), attributing them to potential non-human intelligence while ruling out human hoaxing based on pattern complexity and soil analysis.
The organization maintains skepticism toward extraterrestrial hypotheses, often correlating sightings with meteorological events or [military](https://grokipedia.com/page/Military) tests, yet persists in cataloging unexplained cases amid Russia's history of high-volume reports, including the 1977 Petrozavodsk phenomenon initially misidentified as a [satellite](https://grokipedia.com/page/Satellite) deployment. [Kosmopoisk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kosmopoisk) has expanded to international collaborations, deploying tools like UfoSETI for monitoring and data analysis of aerial anomalies.
Other entities include the International Information Center for UFO Investigation in [Saint Petersburg](https://grokipedia.com/page/Saint_Petersburg), led by Valery Uvarov, which compiles reports of close encounters and [physical evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Physical_Evidence) from Russian territory dating back to Soviet archives. Uvarov's work emphasizes declassified military data, positing some incidents as interactions with advanced propulsion systems rather than conventional explanations.
Post-Soviet state interest waned, with the [Russian Academy of Sciences](https://grokipedia.com/page/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences) occasionally reviewing historical sightings through [ad hoc](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ad_hoc) commissions rather than dedicated bodies, as noted in 2021 statements on ongoing analyses of global UFO patterns without endorsing alien origins. Civilian groups like [Kosmopoisk](https://grokipedia.com/page/Kosmopoisk) fill this gap, though empirical verification remains challenged by misidentification rates exceeding 90% in declassified Soviet-era studies attributing phenomena to rocket exhausts and [atmospheric optics](https://grokipedia.com/page/Atmospheric_optics).
### British Organizations

The **British UFO Research Association (BUFORA)**, established in 1962 through the federation of several regional UFO research groups and individuals, serves as the longest-standing national body in the [United Kingdom](https://grokipedia.com/page/United_Kingdom) focused on the empirical investigation of unidentified aerial phenomena. Operating as a non-profit organization with a [code of conduct](https://grokipedia.com/page/Code_of_conduct) emphasizing scientific evaluation over speculative or cultist interpretations, BUFORA maintains a network of trained field investigators who document and analyze public reports of sightings, excluding those attributable to conventional explanations such as [aircraft](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aircraft) or meteorological events. The group publishes findings via newsletters and hosts annual conferences, such as the 2012 event covered by [BBC News](https://grokipedia.com/page/BBC_News), where members discussed ongoing cases without endorsing extraterrestrial hypotheses absent verifiable evidence. As of 2024, BUFORA coordinates nationwide reports and positions itself as the sole national UFO research entity in the UK, prioritizing data collection over advocacy.
**British Ufology**, formalized in 2015 from an informal UFO club originating in the 1980s, conducts scientific-oriented studies of UFO encounters across [England](https://grokipedia.com/page/England), [Scotland](https://grokipedia.com/page/Scotland), and [Wales](https://grokipedia.com/page/Wales). The organization emphasizes rigorous analysis of witness testimonies and photographic evidence, maintaining independence from broader [paranormal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Paranormal) claims while archiving cases for [pattern recognition](https://grokipedia.com/page/Pattern_recognition). It operates without affiliation to international bodies, focusing on UK-specific data to assess potential causal factors like misidentifications or rare atmospheric phenomena.
Regional groups supplement national efforts, including the **Welsh UFO Research Network (WUFORN)**, which investigates sightings in [Wales](https://grokipedia.com/page/Wales) with confidentiality protocols for reporters, and **Contact UFO Research**, active as of 2024 in soliciting detailed accounts for cataloging without presuming non-human origins. Smaller entities, such as the **Monmouth UFO Group (MUFOG)** in [Wales](https://grokipedia.com/page/Wales), hold monthly discussions on reported phenomena but lack national scope. Defunct organizations include the **British Flying Saucer Bureau**, which ceased operations prior to [2005](https://grokipedia.com/page/2005) after early post-war investigations. These groups collectively reflect a [UK](https://grokipedia.com/page/United_Kingdom) tradition of civilian-led inquiry, often skeptical of media [sensationalism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sensationalism) and government disclosures, prioritizing verifiable eyewitness data over unconfirmed narratives.
### Other European Organizations

In Germany, the **Centrales Erforschungsnetz außergewöhnlicher Himmelsphänomene (CENAP)**, founded in 1976 by Werner Walter and Hansjürgen Köhler following Walter's 1973 UFO sighting, functions as a research network investigating anomalous aerial phenomena through empirical analysis and witness interviews, maintaining a [hotline](https://grokipedia.com/page/Hotline) that has logged over 10,000 reports since inception, with 800 received in 2023 alone, emphasizing prosaic explanations over extraterrestrial hypotheses.
In [Italy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Italy), the **Centro Italiano Studi Ufologici (CISU)**, formed in 1985 by consolidating regional UFO study groups, operates as a nonprofit entity archiving sightings, publishing the bimonthly *UFO – Rivista di Informazione Ufologica*, and conducting cataloged investigations into Italian cases dating back decades, including radar-visual encounters and photographic evidence, while prioritizing data verification over speculative interpretations.
In [Belgium](https://grokipedia.com/page/Belgium), the **Société Belge d'Étude des Phénomènes Spatiaux (SOBEPS)**, established in the 1970s and active until approximately 2003, specialized in field investigations of UFO reports, most prominently the 1989–1990 wave of triangular objects sighted by over 13,500 witnesses, some corroborated by ground radar and pursued by F-16 fighters on March 30–31, 1990, with SOBEPS analyses deeming a subset defying conventional aircraft or atmospheric explanations.
In the [Netherlands](https://grokipedia.com/page/Netherlands), the **UAP Coalition Netherlands**, a recent nonprofit initiative targeting [aviation, law](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aviation_law) enforcement, and [military personnel](https://grokipedia.com/page/Military_personnel) with direct unidentified anomalous phenomena encounters, facilitates anonymous reporting, data aggregation, and interdisciplinary review to distinguish anomalous events from misidentifications, drawing on professional testimonies without presuming non-human origins.
## North American Organizations

### Canadian Organizations

Ufology Research, based in [Winnipeg](https://grokipedia.com/page/Winnipeg), [Manitoba](https://grokipedia.com/page/Manitoba), serves as the primary civilian organization investigating unidentified flying objects (UFOs) in [Canada](https://grokipedia.com/page/Canada). Directed by science writer Chris Rutkowski, it compiles and analyzes public reports through the annual *Canadian UFO Survey*, which has catalogued over 24,000 sightings since 1989, with 570 documented in 2023. The group identifies patterns in reports, such as correlations with [population density](https://grokipedia.com/page/Population_density) and clear [weather](https://grokipedia.com/page/Weather), and estimates an average of three sightings reported daily across the country. Its work emphasizes empirical classification, with most cases attributed to misidentifications of aircraft, balloons, or natural phenomena rather than extraordinary origins.
Historical Canadian UFO groups emerged in the mid-20th century amid increased public interest following global sightings. The Canadian Aerial Phenomena Research Organization, active in [Winnipeg](https://grokipedia.com/page/Winnipeg), [Manitoba](https://grokipedia.com/page/Manitoba), focused on collecting and evaluating witness accounts during the [1960s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1960s) and [1970s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1970s). Similarly, Canadian UFO Research operated from [Oshawa](https://grokipedia.com/page/Oshawa), [Ontario](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ontario), conducting fieldwork and report verification in the same era. Other regional entities included the Toronto UFO Research Group, which documented urban sightings, and the UFO International Information Centre in [Vancouver](https://grokipedia.com/page/Vancouver), which disseminated information and encouraged reporting. These groups largely disbanded by the [1980s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1980s) due to lack of funding and waning volunteer participation, though their archives contributed to later compilations.
Government-led efforts paralleled civilian initiatives, notably Project Magnet, initiated in 1950 by radio engineer Wilbert B. Smith under the Department of Transport. This program instrumentally monitored potential electromagnetic anomalies associated with UFOs but was terminated in 1954 after inconclusive results and official skepticism toward extraterrestrial hypotheses. Academic projects, such as the [University of Toronto](https://grokipedia.com/page/University_of_Toronto) Institute for Aerospace Studies (UTIAS) UFO Project in the 1960s under Dr. Gordon Patterson, examined sightings scientifically but ended without establishing novel causal mechanisms beyond conventional explanations.
Provincial branches of international networks, like [British Columbia](https://grokipedia.com/page/British_Columbia) MUFON, continue localized investigations, training field investigators to interview witnesses and rule out prosaic causes such as drones or satellites. Overall, Canadian UFO organizations prioritize [data aggregation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Data_aggregation) over speculative theories, reflecting a cautious approach informed by verifiable witness testimonies and environmental factors.
### Active U.S. Organizations

The [Mutual UFO Network](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mutual_UFO_Network) (MUFON), founded in 1969 and headquartered in [Cincinnati](https://grokipedia.com/page/Cincinnati), [Ohio](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ohio), operates as a nonprofit dedicated to the scientific investigation of UFO sightings, abductions, and related phenomena through a network of field investigators who analyze reports using protocols including witness interviews, photographic evaluation, and physical evidence collection. As of 2025, MUFON maintains an active case management system, processing hundreds of reports monthly; for instance, it documented robust UFO activity in the first seven months of the year, though outnumbered by the National UFO Reporting Center's totals. The organization publishes the MUFON Journal, with the October 2025 issue covering ongoing analyses, and engages in public education via symposia and media appearances, including expert testimony on non-human technology indicators during congressional hearings. 
The [National UFO Reporting Center](https://grokipedia.com/page/National_UFO_Reporting_Center) (NUFORC), established in 1974 and based in [Seattle](https://grokipedia.com/page/Seattle), Washington, functions as a centralized database for public-submitted UFO/UAP reports, emphasizing transparency by making sightings, images, videos, and maps publicly accessible without initial vetting for credibility. In the first half of 2025 alone, NUFORC received over 3,000 reports, with more than 2,000 UFO sightings logged, highlighting a surge attributed partly to reduced stigma following government disclosures, though the center notes underreporting remains likely due to persistent skepticism. It avoids formal investigations, focusing instead on aggregation to support researchers, and has been cited in analyses of U.S. sighting hotspots correlating with low-light-pollution areas.
The Center for UFO Studies (CUFOS), founded in 1973 by astronomer [J. Allen Hynek](https://grokipedia.com/page/J._Allen_Hynek) in [Chicago](https://grokipedia.com/page/Chicago), [Illinois](https://grokipedia.com/page/Illinois), serves as a privately funded [archive](https://grokipedia.com/page/Archive) and research entity preserving UFO case files, periodicals, and artifacts while conducting scholarly inquiries into unexplained aerial phenomena. It remains operational, prioritizing empirical review over [sensationalism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sensationalism), with holdings including thousands of documented cases analyzed for patterns in [radar](https://grokipedia.com/page/Radar), photographic, and eyewitness data.
Americans for Safe Aerospace (ASA), a nonprofit launched in 2023 by former Navy pilot Ryan Graves and headquartered in [Virginia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Virginia), concentrates on UAP encounters posing risks to [aviation safety](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aviation_safety) and [national security](https://grokipedia.com/page/National_security), advocating for standardized reporting, pilot support, and data-driven [policy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Policy) through [witness](https://grokipedia.com/page/Witness) advocacy and public [education](https://grokipedia.com/page/Education) initiatives. ASA's mission includes fostering [collaboration](https://grokipedia.com/page/Collaboration) among aviators, researchers, and officials, with Graves testifying before [Congress](https://grokipedia.com/page/Congress) in 2023 on near-miss incidents involving unidentified objects exhibiting advanced maneuvers. As of 2025, it actively solicits UAP sightings from [aircrew](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aircrew) and promotes transparency to mitigate hazards, distinct from broader extraterrestrial hypotheses by emphasizing verifiable flight safety data.
### Defunct U.S. Organizations

The National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena (NICAP) was established on October 24, 1956, in [Washington, D.C.](https://grokipedia.com/page/Washington,_D.C.), as a civilian nonprofit dedicated to scientific investigation of UFO reports, advocacy for government disclosure, and countering perceived official secrecy. Led initially by Major Donald E. Keyhoe, a retired Marine Corps aviator, NICAP amassed thousands of sighting cases, published analyses like the 1960 "The UFO Evidence" report citing over 300 quality sightings unexplained by the [U.S. Air Force](https://grokipedia.com/page/Air_force), and maintained a network of investigators including astronomers, pilots, and [scientists](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_Scientists). Internal conflicts, leadership turnover—such as John Acuff's 1970 appointment amid financial strains—and declining membership amid shifting [public interest](https://grokipedia.com/page/Public_interest) led to its effective dissolution by the late [1970s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1970s), though archives persist for [research](https://grokipedia.com/page/Research).
The [Aerial Phenomena Research Organization](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aerial_Phenomena_Research_Organization) (APRO), founded in January 1952 by Jim and Coral Lorenzen in [Albuquerque, New Mexico](https://grokipedia.com/page/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico), operated as one of the earliest international civilian UFO research groups, emphasizing field investigations, witness interviews, and publication of the bimonthly APRO Bulletin documenting global cases. With a focus on physical evidence and consulting experts like physicists, APRO cataloged over 10,000 reports by the 1970s, including early [contactee](https://grokipedia.com/page/Contactee) claims and radar-visual sightings, and influenced policy through congressional testimonies. Operations ceased in late 1988 following Coral Lorenzen's death on April 11 of that year, exacerbated by funding shortages and competition from newer groups like MUFON, which absorbed some members and files after APRO's split in 1969.
Civilian Saucer Intelligence (CSI), formed in 1954 in [New York City](https://grokipedia.com/page/New_York_City) by ufologists including Isabel L. Davis and Norman J. Glick, functioned as an early independent group analyzing "[flying saucer](https://grokipedia.com/page/Flying_saucer)" sightings through newsletters and case compilations, prioritizing skeptical scrutiny of hoaxes while documenting credible aerial anomalies. It issued the [CSI News Letter](https://grokipedia.com/page/News_Letter) from 1954 to 1959, covering over 100 U.S. reports with emphasis on witness credibility and correlation with radar data, but disbanded shortly thereafter due to limited resources and volunteer burnout in the pre-institutionalized UFO research era.
Other smaller entities, such as Citizens Against UFO Secrecy (CAUS), active in the 1970s-1980s advocating legal action for declassification under FOIA, and the Fund for UFO Research (FUFOR), which granted funds for investigations until the early [2000s](https://grokipedia.com/page/2000s), also folded amid waning donations and unresolved evidential challenges in civilian [ufology](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ufology). These groups collectively advanced private-sector UFO documentation before larger survivors like MUFON dominated, though their archives reveal persistent patterns of unexplained aerial observations resistant to prosaic explanations.
## South American Organizations

### Brazilian Organizations

The **Comissão Brasileira de Ufólogos (CBU)**, established in 1997 by prominent ufologist Ademar Gevaerd following the I Fórum Mundial de Ufologia, functions as a national advocacy group for ufological research and transparency in government-held UFO records. The CBU has spearheaded campaigns such as "UFOs: Liberdade de Informação," pressing Brazilian authorities, including the Ministry of Defense, to declassify documents on UFO sightings dating back to events like the 1977-1978 Operação Prato investigation by the Brazilian Air Force. Membership includes researchers like co-editors Marco Antonio Petit and Claudeir Covo, who collaborate with the Revista UFO publication to disseminate case studies and analysis.
Regional and specialized groups supplement national efforts, such as the **Grupo de Estudos e Pesquisas Ufológicas Rio Preto (GEPURP)**, based in [São José do Rio Preto](https://grokipedia.com/page/S%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_do_Rio_Preto), which investigates local sightings and hosts events for enthusiasts. Similarly, **Ufologia Integral Brasil (UIB)** emphasizes integrated casuistic, scientific, and spiritual approaches to UFO phenomena, maintaining an online presence for study and discussion without conducting image or video analyses. Earlier entities like the **Grupo Paracientífico Brasil (GEB)**, active in the early [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s), contributed to initial ufological periodicals but appear defunct or less prominent today.
Brazilian ufology intersects with official military documentation, as the [Air Force](https://grokipedia.com/page/Air_force) has recorded over 700 UFO incidents since the [1950s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1950s), though civilian organizations like the CBU focus on public access rather than operational investigations. These groups operate amid a [cultural landscape](https://grokipedia.com/page/Cultural_landscape) rich in sightings, including the 1986 [Official](https://grokipedia.com/page/Official) UFO Night involving 21 objects tracked by radar, yet face challenges from limited funding and reliance on witness testimonies over empirical instrumentation.
### Other South American Organizations

In Argentina, the Commission for the Study of Aerospace Phenomena (CEFAE), established in May 2011 within the [Argentine Air Force](https://grokipedia.com/page/Argentine_Air_Force), was tasked with investigating UFO sightings and related reports on national territory.  The agency analyzed nearly 40 reported sightings, attributing most to conventional explanations such as [aircraft](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aircraft) or atmospheric phenomena, before being disbanded in [February](https://grokipedia.com/page/February) 2016 amid criticisms of inactivity and unsubstantiated findings. 
In [Chile](https://grokipedia.com/page/Chile), the Committee for the Study of Anomalous Aerial Phenomena (CEFAA), formed in 1997 under the [Directorate General](https://grokipedia.com/page/Directorate-General) of Civil [Aeronautics](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aeronautics), serves as the official body for examining unidentified aerial phenomena, including pilot and [radar](https://grokipedia.com/page/Radar) reports. The organization has declassified videos and images, such as a 2014 Navy helicopter sighting of an infrared object exhibiting unusual [thermal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Thermal) signatures and flight behavior unexplained by birds, debris, or aircraft. 
Peru maintains a government program for UFO investigations, with efforts including the reopening of an official office in [2014](https://grokipedia.com/page/2014) to handle sightings and anomalous reports, though operational details and specific outcomes remain limited in public records. 
In Uruguay, the Commission for the Reception and Investigation of Reports of Unidentified Flying Objects (CRIDOVNI), created on August 7, 1979, by the [Uruguayan Air Force](https://grokipedia.com/page/Uruguayan_Air_Force), actively processes sighting complaints, conducts witness interviews, and collaborates with [air traffic control](https://grokipedia.com/page/Air_traffic_control) to rule out prosaic causes.  The commission, comprising seven permanent members as of 2023, has investigated incidents like flashing red lights over [Paysandú](https://grokipedia.com/page/Paysand%C3%BA) in February 2023.  Complementing this, the Uruguayan UFO Investigation Center (CIOVI), a [civilian](https://grokipedia.com/page/Civilian) non-profit founded on April 29, 1958, focuses on independent research into sightings and related phenomena.
## Asian Organizations

### Japanese Organizations

The **Japan Flying Saucer Research Association** (日本飛行円盤研究会, *Nihon Hikō Enban Kenkyukai*), founded in 1955 by ufologist Arai Kin'ichi, was the first organized group dedicated to investigating unidentified flying objects in [Japan](https://grokipedia.com/page/Japan). The association collected sighting reports and promoted rational inquiry into aerial phenomena, attracting early enthusiasts including author [Yukio Mishima](https://grokipedia.com/page/Yukio_Mishima), who joined in 1956 despite his personal skepticism about extraterrestrial origins. It published bulletins and organized discussions amid post-World War II interest in anomalous sky events, though its activities waned by the late [20th century](https://grokipedia.com/page/20th_century), rendering it largely defunct.
The **Cosmic Brotherhood Association** (宇宙兄弟会, *Uchū Kyōdai Kai*), active from the late [1950s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1950s), represented a more esoteric branch of Japanese [ufology](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ufology) by advocating direct communication with extraterrestrial beings via [telepathy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Telepathy) and ritual chanting of the [mantra](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mantra) "Bentra, Bentra." Unlike evidence-focused groups, it emphasized spiritual contact and integration with alien "brothers," aligning with [contactee](https://grokipedia.com/page/Contactee) movements; it affiliated with the Japan Flying Saucer Research Association around 1960 and issued monthly publications like *The Flying Saucer*. The group's claims of successful interstellar rapport lacked empirical verification and drew from broader [New Age](https://grokipedia.com/page/New_Age) influences rather than scientific methodology.
In response to persistent regional sightings, the **International UFO Laboratory** opened on June 24, 2021, in Iino, Fukushima Prefecture, operating within the UFO Interactive Hall to catalog and analyze unexplained aerial incidents, particularly those reported since the 1970s in the "UFO Village" area. Supported by [local government](https://grokipedia.com/page/Local_government) for [tourism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Tourism) and [research](https://grokipedia.com/page/Research), it maintains archives of witness testimonies and physical evidence claims, though investigations prioritize prosaic explanations like misidentified [aircraft](https://grokipedia.com/page/Aircraft) over extraterrestrial hypotheses. This facility reflects Japan's recent surge in ufological interest, spurred by U.S. disclosures, but operates as a semi-official outpost rather than an independent advocacy body.

In China, the China UFO Research Organization (CURO), established in 1978, was the country's initial formal body for investigating unidentified flying objects, later evolving into the China UFO Research Association (CURA) by 1980 with branches in multiple provinces. CURA focused on scientific analysis of sightings, including verifiable observation reports, and maintained ties to the [China](https://grokipedia.com/page/China) Association for Science and Technology, though activities faced increasing restrictions post-2010s amid broader suppression of fringe groups.
India hosts several dedicated UFO research entities, including the TOP Research Group, which claims to be the nation's first organization focused on aliens and UFOs, compiling details on sightings and related phenomena through public reports and investigations. The IARF Centre of Excellence for UFOs (ICEU), launched in 2021, builds a searchable database of UFO reports from Indian media and conducts primary surveys to document aerial anomalies. Additionally, the Alien & UFO Research Organisation of India (AURO) analyzes and documents extraterrestrial evidence, positioning itself as a pioneer in systematic UFO study within the country.
In [Indonesia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Indonesia), BETA-UFO, founded in 1997, operates as the largest UFO community, with over 19,000 registered members engaged in sighting investigations and public education, emphasizing non-cult status and empirical approaches to unexplained aerial events.
The UFO Society of the [Philippines](https://grokipedia.com/page/Philippines) (UFOSP), formed in 2010, serves as the first membership-based formal organization in the country, dedicated to researching and archiving UFO encounters through member-submitted data and fieldwork.
South Korea's Korea UAP Society (한국 UAP 학회) promotes sharing of UFO and UAP evidence to enhance public understanding, operating as a platform for new findings and discussions on anomalous phenomena.
Malaysia features informal communities like the UFO Malaysia Community, which networks enthusiasts for reporting and debating sightings, though lacking a centralized formal structure comparable to neighbors.

### Australian Organizations

UFO Research (NSW) Incorporated, formed in 1991 and based in [Sydney](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sydney), functions as a non-profit entity dedicated to UFO and extraterrestrial research, welcoming public reports of sightings and hosting monthly meetings to discuss evidence and investigations. ## African Organizations

### South African Organizations

South African UFO organizations primarily consist of small, independent civilian groups focused on collecting eyewitness reports of unidentified aerial phenomena, with limited institutional support or scientific endorsement. The South African UFO Resource (SAUFOR), founded by researcher Cristo Louw, serves as a key repository for UFO sighting data in the country. ## Skeptical and Debunking Organizations

### Active Skeptical Groups

The **Committee for Skeptical Inquiry (CSI)**, established in 1976, remains a leading active organization applying [scientific skepticism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Scientific_skepticism) to UFO claims, emphasizing [empirical evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Empirical_evidence) over anecdotal reports or unsubstantiated extraterrestrial hypotheses. Affiliated with the Center for Inquiry (CFI), CSI publishes the bimonthly *Skeptical Inquirer* magazine, which regularly features peer-reviewed analyses debunking UFO sightings as misidentifications of conventional aircraft, atmospheric phenomena, or optical illusions. For example, CSI investigations have identified many "radar-visual" UFO cases as prosaic explanations, such as radar anomalies or distant aircraft, converting UFOs to identified flying objects (IFOs).
CSI's ongoing work includes public statements cautioning against [sensationalism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sensationalism) in UFO reporting; in 2021, following U.S. government UAP assessments, the organization stressed that "a UFO doesn't necessarily mean aliens," urging reliance on verifiable data rather than [speculation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Speculation). Members like [astronomer](https://grokipedia.com/page/Astronomer) James McGaha continue to contribute detailed case studies, such as balloon-related misperceptions mimicking UFO maneuvers, reinforcing that extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence absent in most reports.
Other active skeptical entities, such as CSI's international branches and collaborations, extend this scrutiny globally, hosting conferences and workshops that dissect UFO evidence using tools like video analysis and atmospheric modeling to attribute sightings to earthly causes. These groups prioritize [falsifiability](https://grokipedia.com/page/Falsifiability) and replication, contrasting with belief-oriented UFO communities by documenting how [confirmation bias](https://grokipedia.com/page/Confirmation_bias) and cultural influences amplify unverified narratives.
### Defunct Skeptical Groups

The United States Air Force's **Project Sign**, initiated in 1947 as the first formal UFO investigation, initially explored the possibility of extraterrestrial origins for sightings but ultimately rejected it in favor of prosaic explanations, disbanding in early 1949 after analyzing early post-World War II reports. Its successor, **Project Grudge** (1949–1952), adopted a more explicitly skeptical posture, aiming to systematically debunk UFO reports through scientific scrutiny; it reviewed hundreds of cases, attributing most to misidentifications of conventional aircraft, balloons, or natural phenomena, before transitioning into [Project Blue Book](https://grokipedia.com/page/Project_Blue_Book).
**Project Blue Book**, active from 1952 to 1969, represented the Air Force's longest-running UFO inquiry, cataloging 12,618 sightings with approximately 701 (about 5.6%) remaining unidentified after investigation. Staffed by astronomers, engineers, and analysts, it emphasized rational explanations—such as hoaxes, optical illusions, or classified military tests—concluding in its final 1969 report that UFOs posed no [national security](https://grokipedia.com/page/National_security) threat and lacked evidence of advanced technology or extraterrestrial visitation, leading to its termination amid public pressure and budget constraints.
The **Condon Committee**, formally the University of Colorado's [Scientific Study](https://grokipedia.com/page/Scientific_study) of Unidentified Flying Objects (1966–1968), was a civilian academic effort funded by the [Air Force](https://grokipedia.com/page/Air_force) to assess the scientific merit of UFO research. Led by [physicist](https://grokipedia.com/page/Physicist) Edward U. Condon, the team examined 59 cases in depth, applying rigorous empirical methods and finding no compelling evidence for anomalous phenomena warranting further study; the 1968 report recommended ending government-sponsored UFO investigations, influencing Project Blue Book's closure and reflecting a consensus among involved scientists that sightings were attributable to psychological, environmental, or technological mundane causes.
