# AARO (All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office)

> **Summary:** The All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) is a U.S. Department of Defense organization established in July 2022 to centralize investigations into unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP) across all domains (air, sea, land, space, and transmedium). Building on predecessor bodies including the UAP Task Force, AARO reviews historical and current UAP cases, coordinates with intelligence agencies, and reports to Congress. Its March 2024 Historical Record Report concluded there is no verifiable evidence of extraterrestrial technology, reverse-engineering programs, or government cover-ups across eight decades of reviewed cases. Its November 2024 Consolidated Annual Report documented over 1,600 total cases, with most resolved as prosaic objects (balloons, drones, birds, satellites) and 21 remaining "true anomalies" lacking multi-sensor data for resolution.

## Establishment and Mandate

AARO was established within the Department of Defense in July 2022, building on earlier UAP investigation bodies:

1. **[Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP)](../events/aatip.md):** A secret Pentagon program from 2007 to 2012, funded with an initial $22 million allocation, focused on analyzing anomalous aerospace threats. Its existence was publicly revealed in December 2017 by *The New York Times*, alongside three declassified Navy videos (FLIR1, Gimbal, GoFast).
2. **UAP Task Force (UAPTF):** Established in 2020 under the Office of Naval Intelligence to formalize UAP investigation and reporting.
3. **AARO:** Created to centralize and expand these efforts across all military domains, with Congressional reporting requirements.

AARO's mandate includes:
- Receiving and analyzing UAP reports from military personnel, intelligence agencies, and (increasingly) commercial aviation
- Reviewing historical U.S. government UAP investigations dating to 1945
- Coordinating with the Intelligence Community and allied nations
- Reporting findings to Congress and the public

## Key Findings

### Historical Record Report, Volume 1 (March 8, 2024)

AARO's most comprehensive publication examined U.S. government UAP investigations since 1945, including reviews of programs like [Project Sign](../events/project-blue-book.md), Project Grudge, Project Blue Book, AATIP, and various intelligence community activities. Core conclusions:

- **No verifiable evidence** of extraterrestrial technology has been identified
- **No evidence** of reverse-engineering programs involving non-human craft
- **No evidence** of government cover-ups of extraterrestrial contact
- Many historical claims were attributed to misidentifications, hoaxes, or classified human technology programs
- Cases popularized by figures like [Jeremy Corbell](jeremy-corbell.md) were addressed, with AARO emphasizing misperception over paradigm-shifting phenomena

### Consolidated Annual Report on UAP (November 14, 2024)

Documented over 1,600 cases received since 2021:
- **Most cases resolved** as balloons, drones, birds, satellites, or sensor artifacts
- **21 incidents** from fiscal year 2024 remained anomalous due to lacking multi-sensor data
- **Increased reporting** from commercial sectors noted
- **No extraterrestrial findings** reiterated
- Reports describe phenomena such as unidentified lights, orb-shaped objects, and spherical or cylindrical forms, with no verified instances of humanoid entities

### June 2021 ODNI Preliminary Assessment (Predecessor Report)

The Office of the Director of National Intelligence analyzed 144 UAP reports from U.S. military personnel (2004-2021) and categorized cases into five potential explanations:
1. Airborne clutter
2. Natural phenomena
3. U.S. or industry developmental programs
4. Foreign adversary systems
5. "Other"

143 incidents remained unexplained due to insufficient data, though the report emphasized no conclusive evidence of extraterrestrial origins while highlighting risks to flight safety and national security.

## Relationship to Congressional Oversight

AARO operates within a framework of increasing Congressional scrutiny of UAP:

- **May 17, 2022:** Joint House and Senate session where Pentagon officials testified to 400+ UAP reports
- **July 26, 2023:** House Oversight Committee hearing featuring whistleblower David Grusch, who alleged U.S. possession of non-human craft and "biologics" (he provided no direct evidence and confirmed he had not personally viewed such materials)
- **November 13, 2024:** House Committee on Oversight and Accountability hearing "Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena: Exposing the Truth," featuring testimony referencing the alleged "Immaculate Constellation" program
- **September 9, 2025:** House Oversight task force session criticizing federal transparency gaps

Proposed legislation includes H.R.1187 (UAP Transparency Act), seeking mandatory declassification of UAP records.

## Criticisms

### From Disclosure Advocates

[Jeremy Corbell](jeremy-corbell.md) published a 2025 open letter criticizing AARO's transparency and treatment of whistleblowers, demanding:
- Full release of 2017 "Jellyfish" UAP footage from Lake Habbaniyah, Iraq
- Denunciation of alleged slander against whistleblowers David Grusch and Matthew Brown

Congressional representatives have criticized AARO for insufficient transparency and potential institutional resistance to genuine anomaly investigation.

### From Skeptics

Some skeptics argue AARO's establishment risks legitimizing fringe claims by treating them as worthy of government resources, though the office's repeated findings of no extraterrestrial evidence counter this concern.

## Relationship to Other UAP Bodies

- **NASA Independent UAP Study Team (reported September 14, 2023):** Found no evidence linking UAP to extraterrestrial life; recommended rigorous scientific protocols for data collection
- **[MUFON](mufon.md) and [NUFORC](nuforc.md):** Civilian organizations that complement AARO's work with broader public reporting; AARO's analytical rigor exceeds civilian capabilities
- **Americans for Safe Aerospace (ASA):** Founded 2023 by former Navy pilot Ryan Graves, focuses specifically on aviation safety dimensions of UAP; advocates for standardized military reporting to AARO
