# Luis Elizondo

> **Summary:** Luis Elizondo is a former U.S. intelligence official who directed the Pentagon's Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP) from 2007 to 2012. He resigned in October 2017, citing excessive secrecy and bureaucratic resistance to UAP transparency, and became the central figure in the December 2017 New York Times disclosure that revealed AATIP's existence to the public. Elizondo's actions reframed UFOs from fringe speculation into a national security concern, catalyzing congressional inquiries, Pentagon video authentications, and the eventual establishment of AARO.

## Role in AATIP

The Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP) was initiated in 2007 under the Defense Intelligence Agency, with approximately $22 million allocated through 2012 to investigate unidentified aerial phenomena as potential national security threats. The program was established at the behest of Senate Majority Leader Harry Reid and involved contractors such as Bigelow Aerospace.

Elizondo directed [AATIP](../events/aatip.md)'s operations within the Office of the Under Secretary of Defense for Intelligence. The program focused on gathering data from military sensor systems, including radar, infrared, and electro-optical recordings of objects exhibiting hypersonic speeds, sudden maneuvers defying known aerodynamics, and transmedium capabilities without visible propulsion signatures. A pivotal case investigated under AATIP was the November 2004 [USS Nimitz encounter](../events/uss-nimitz-2004.md), where F/A-18 pilots observed a Tic Tac-shaped object demonstrating rapid acceleration and descent from 80,000 feet to sea level in seconds, corroborated by ship-based radar and FLIR video footage.

Elizondo asserted that AATIP's analysis ruled out U.S. or allied technology for the observed phenomena, attributing them to potential advanced threats warranting further empirical scrutiny.

## 2017 Resignation and Disclosure

Elizondo resigned from the Pentagon in October 2017, citing excessive secrecy and bureaucratic resistance to transparency as impeding effective threat assessment. Following his departure, the December 16, 2017, New York Times article revealed AATIP's existence and released the FLIR, Gimbal, and GoFast videos, marking what sources describe as a paradigm shift that reframed UFOs (later termed UAP) from fringe speculation to national security imperatives.

The disclosure emphasized incursions by objects outperforming U.S. aircraft in speed, stealth, and maneuverability, prompting congressional inquiries and Pentagon acknowledgments of unexplained events near sensitive sites. Pentagon spokesperson Christopher Sherwood confirmed AATIP's existence but stated it had concluded in 2012.

## To The Stars Academy

In 2017, Elizondo joined To The Stars Academy (TTSA), founded by musician Tom DeLonge with former intelligence officials, which publicized declassified U.S. Navy videos of unidentified aerial objects and pursued research into advanced aerospace technologies. TTSA secured a U.S. Army contract in 2019 for materials research inspired by UAP observations but shifted focus toward entertainment and broader science initiatives by the early 2020s.

## Advocacy and Public Statements

Elizondo has consistently advocated for government transparency on UAP. In a 2021 Washington Post interview, he detailed how AATIP analyzed military sensor data revealing objects outperforming F-22 Raptors, with no evidence of conventional explanations such as drones or balloons. He highlighted the report's documentation of 18 incidents involving UAP that demonstrated unusual movement patterns: remaining stationary in high winds, moving rapidly against wind resistance, performing abrupt maneuvers, and exhibiting high speeds without discernible propulsion signatures.

Elizondo interpreted these observables (acceleration without visible exhaust, hypersonic velocities without sonic booms, and apparent signature management) as indicative of technology beyond known human capabilities. He advocated for transparency, claiming government possession of verifiable UAP evidence while cautioning against premature extraterrestrial conclusions without full disclosure.

## Criticisms and Official Responses

Independent analysts, including video debunker Mick West, have replicated many associated UAP videos using commercial software to demonstrate mundane causes such as sensor artifacts and parallax illusions. The Pentagon's [AARO](../entities/aaro.md), established in 2022, reviewed historical UAP claims and found no verifiable evidence of extraterrestrial technology or secret reverse-engineering programs in its March 2024 Historical Record Report. AARO attributed persistent claims to circular reporting, cultural influences, and misinterpretations of classified projects.

The scientific consensus views the "unidentified" category as an invitation for empirical scrutiny rather than validation of non-human intelligence.
