# GEIPAN

> **Summary:** GEIPAN (Groupe d'Etudes et d'Informations sur les Phenomenes Aerospatiaux Non-identifies) is the French government's official body for investigating unidentified aerospace phenomena, operated under the national space agency CNES. Established in 1977 as GEPAN and reorganized under its current name in 2005, GEIPAN has processed approximately 8,000 reports, classifying them from identified (Category A) to unexplained (Category D). Roughly 3 to 5 percent of cases remain unexplained after rigorous scrutiny, though the unit emphasizes prosaic explanations over speculative hypotheses.

## History and Organizational Evolution

France is notable among nations for maintaining a continuous, government-sanctioned UFO investigation body for nearly five decades:

- **GEPAN (1977-1988):** The Groupe d'Etude des Phenomenes Aerospatiaux Non-identifies was established in 1977 under CNES, France's national space agency, to collect, analyze, and archive reports of unidentified aerospace phenomena. GEPAN handled initial official inquiries into over 1,000 cases.
- **SEPRA (1999-2004):** An intermediate organizational phase during efforts to centralize reporting.
- **GEIPAN (2005-present):** The current iteration, which serves as the primary public-facing organization for UAP studies in France.

Unlike most nations, where UFO investigation has been handled through military or intelligence channels before being discontinued, France institutionalized the effort within its civilian space agency, reflecting a commitment to systematic data collection rather than dismissal.

## Methodology and Classification System

GEIPAN classifies cases using a graduated system:

- **Category A:** Identified with certainty (e.g., aircraft, balloons, atmospheric phenomena)
- **Category B:** Probably identified
- **Category C:** Insufficient data for classification
- **Category D:** Unexplained despite thorough investigation

The unit's methodology prioritizes empirical data from witnesses, radar records, and photographs. GEIPAN collaborates with a network of regional investigators for fieldwork and employs four full-time staff alongside volunteers.

As of 2023, GEIPAN maintains a database of approximately 8,000 reports spanning cases since 1937. About 3 to 5 percent of cases remain unexplained after rigorous scrutiny (sources cite both 3% and 5% for different time periods, with a separate figure of approximately 22% of over 2,000 cases cited for Category D classification, reflecting different methodological criteria over the decades).

## Notable Investigations

The 1981 Trans-en-Provence case represents one of GEIPAN's most significant investigations. A farmer witnessed a saucer-shaped object land and depart, compacting soil into a 2.5-meter diameter circle. GEPAN (the predecessor unit) investigated within hours and found the soil had been heated to 300-600 degrees Celsius instantaneously, with altered crystalline structures, elevated calcium, zinc, and phosphorus levels, and nearby plants exhibiting biochemical changes inconsistent with natural degradation. GEPAN classified the case as unexplained.

## Public Transparency

GEIPAN has publicly archived its findings since 2007, making declassified case files accessible to the public. This transparency initiative contrasts with more opaque approaches in other nations and has been a model for [UFO organizations internationally](../entities/ufo-organizations-international.md).

## Relationship to the COMETA Report

The independent COMETA report, authored by French military officers and scientists in 1999, examined global UFO cases and proposed the [extraterrestrial hypothesis](../concepts/extraterrestrial-hypothesis.md) as plausible for a minority of unexplained incidents. GEIPAN has distanced itself from COMETA's ET-leaning conclusions, prioritizing empirical analysis over origin hypotheses. GEIPAN emphasizes prosaic explanations, including optical illusions and equipment failures, over extraordinary hypotheses.
