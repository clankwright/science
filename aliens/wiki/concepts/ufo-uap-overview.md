# UFO/UAP Overview

> **Summary:** An unidentified flying object (UFO), more recently rebranded as an unidentified anomalous phenomenon (UAP), denotes any airborne or transmedium object whose provenance and characteristics elude immediate identification as conventional aircraft, atmospheric artifacts, sensor malfunctions, or prosaic human activities. Systematic scrutiny emerged after World War II, with Project Blue Book cataloging 12,618 reports from 1947 to 1969, of which roughly 701 (5.6%) defied conclusive explanation. Contemporary government assessments, including the 2021 ODNI preliminary report and AARO annual reports through 2025, continue to find most observations lacking sufficient data for resolution while explicitly noting an absence of evidence for extraterrestrial involvement.

## Terminology: UFO, UAP, and the Evolution of Language

Reports of anomalous aerial sightings lacked standardized terminology before the mid-20th century, with observers describing "mysterious lights," "phantom objects," or "unexplained phenomena." During World War II, Allied and Axis pilots encountered luminous orbs dubbed **"foo fighters"**, a term from cartoon slang adopted by aircrews such as the U.S. 415th Night Fighter Squadron.

The phrase **"flying saucer"** emerged on June 24, 1947, following pilot Kenneth Arnold's sighting of nine high-speed objects near Mount Rainier, Washington. Arnold likened their motion to "saucers skipping across the water"; journalists subsequently popularized the term despite many sightings involving varied shapes.

The acronym **"UFO"** (Unidentified Flying Object) was formalized in 1953 by U.S. Air Force Captain Edward J. Ruppelt, who oversaw [Project Blue Book](../events/project-blue-book.md), to replace sensationalized terms and emphasize neutral, technical classification.

By the 2020s, official U.S. entities shifted to **"UAP"** (initially "Unidentified Aerial Phenomena," then broadened to "Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena") to encompass multi-domain detections across air, sea, and space. This terminology was adopted by [NASA](../entities/nasa-uap-study.md) and military protocols by 2022, prioritizing data-driven analysis over speculative narratives.

## Classification Criteria

A sighting qualifies as unidentified when it involves visual or instrumental observation of an airborne object whose nature, origin, or behavior cannot be determined through standard identification procedures despite sufficient credible data. In Project Blue Book's framework, a case was deemed unidentified only if it contained all pertinent details necessary to identify it as a conventional aircraft, celestial body, or atmospheric phenomenon, yet the description defied such explanation.

Classification requires initial elimination of prosaic explanations:
- Commercial or military aircraft, balloons, drones, birds
- Meteorological events (temperature inversions, mirages)
- Optical illusions or sensor artifacts

Reports must exhibit reliability thresholds: corroboration from multiple trained observers, sensor data beyond visual acuity, or physical traces. Single-witness anecdotal accounts with vague details rarely meet the bar.

[AARO](../entities/aaro.md), established in 2022, applies similar standards, resolving most cases as airborne clutter, natural phenomena, or U.S./adversary systems, but retaining as unidentified those defying attribution even with enhanced multi-sensor analysis. France's [GEIPAN](../entities/geipan.md) (since 1977) uses parallel protocols, classifying only 3-5% of vetted cases as "D" (unidentified).

Empirical criteria emphasize quantifiable anomalies:
- **Transmedium travel**: air-to-water transitions without deceleration
- **Hypersonic speeds** without sonic booms
- **Low observability** to radar and infrared while evading countermeasures

## Historical Context

### Ancient and Pre-Modern Reports

Babylonian astronomical tablets (c. 1000 BCE) describe unusual celestial objects. The prophet Ezekiel (c. 593 BCE) depicted "wheels within wheels." Roman historian Livy recorded in 218 BCE that "ships, gleaming and bright, were seen in the sky." Pliny the Elder described a "spark" falling from a star in 76 BCE.

In 1561, Hans Glaser documented a mass sighting over Nuremberg of spheres, cylinders, and crosses. In 1566, Basel residents observed black spheres appearing to battle in the sky. Modern analyses attribute both to parhelia (sundogs), contrails, or ball lightning.

The late 19th century saw **mystery airship** waves (1896-1897) across the U.S., with thousands of witnesses describing cigar-shaped craft with propellers and lights. Most were attributed to hoaxes or misidentified stars and balloons.

### The Modern Era (1947 Onward)

Kenneth Arnold's June 24, 1947, sighting catalyzed the modern UFO era, generating over 800 reports nationwide within months. The [Roswell incident](../events/roswell.md) of July 1947, initially announced as a "flying disc" recovery before retraction as a weather balloon (later identified as Project Mogul), fueled lasting public fascination.

The U.S. Air Force established sequential investigations:
- **[Project Sign](../events/project-blue-book.md)** (1948): 243 reports analyzed
- **Project Grudge** (1949-1951): 244 cases, more skeptical stance
- **[Project Blue Book](../events/project-blue-book.md)** (1952-1969): 12,618 sightings, 701 unidentified

Notable sighting peaks include:
- **1952 Washington, D.C. flap** (July 12-29): Radar stations tracked unidentified targets over restricted airspace at speeds exceeding 7,000 mph. Air Force interceptors scrambled unsuccessfully.
- **1966 Michigan "Swamp Gas" incidents**: Over 1,000 reports that year. J. Allen Hynek's initial "swamp gas" explanation sparked public backlash.
- **1973 global wave**: Tied to geopolitical tensions and the Pascagoula abduction claim.
- **1997 [Phoenix Lights](../events/phoenix-lights.md)**: V-shaped formations witnessed by thousands across Arizona. Partially attributed to military flares but with unresolved leading elements.

## Characteristics of Sightings

### Common Shapes and Descriptions

Analysis of the [NUFORC](../entities/nuforc.md) database (80,000+ cases since 1906) reveals clustering around core forms:

| Shape | Prevalence | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Sphere/Orb | ~40-50% of characterized reports | Perfectly round, often glowing white, orange, or blue |
| [Triangle/Chevron](black-triangles.md) | Second most frequent | Large, dark, silent; prominent from 1980s onward |
| Disc/Saucer | Several thousand reports | Metallic, reflective; 20-50 feet diameter typical |
| Cylinder/Cigar | ~4,000 entries | Elongated, tube-like, metallic gray |
| Tic-Tac | Notable in military encounters | Smooth white capsule; extreme maneuverability |

Reports consistently note absence of conventional aerodynamic features (wings, rotors), with surfaces appearing seamless. A shift from disc dominance in mid-20th-century reports to increased triangular sightings post-1980 has been documented.

### Behavioral Anomalies

The [2021 ODNI preliminary assessment](../events/ufo-report-2021.md) of 144 UAP incidents highlighted 18 cases (21 reports) with anomalous movement patterns corroborated by multiple sensors (radar, infrared, electro-optical, pilot observation):

- **Extreme acceleration**: Estimated 40-700 g-forces in some instances
- **Transmedium capability**: Air-to-water transitions without deceleration (e.g., 2004 [USS Nimitz incident](../events/uss-nimitz-2004.md))
- **No propulsion signatures**: No exhaust plumes, rotor wash, or sonic booms
- **Formation flying and apparent cloaking**: Visual and radar signatures intermittently vanishing despite prior locks

## Key Sensor-Corroborated Cases

**Tehran Incident (September 19, 1976)**: Ground radar at Mehrabad Airport detected an unidentified object; two F-4 Phantom II jets scrambled. The first jet experienced complete instrumentation and communications failure when approaching. The U.S. DIA described it as an "outstanding report" with no conventional explanation.

**[Belgian UFO Wave](../events/belgian-ufo-wave.md) (1989-1990)**: Two F-16 fighters scrambled March 30-31, 1990. Ground radar tracked objects accelerating from 150 to 1,100 mph in seconds, with over 13,000 witnesses including police and civilians.

**[USS Nimitz Encounter](../events/uss-nimitz-2004.md) (November 2004)**: The USS Princeton's SPY-1 radar detected objects descending from 80,000 feet to sea level in under a second over five days. Commander David Fravor visually encountered a white, Tic Tac-shaped object approximately 40 feet long. FLIR footage captured the object without visible propulsion. The Pentagon authenticated the videos in 2020.

## Recent Developments (2017-2025)

The December 2017 *New York Times* disclosure of the [Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP)](../events/aatip.md), a Pentagon initiative allocated approximately $22 million (2007-2012), alongside declassified Navy videos ("FLIR1," "Gimbal," "GoFast"), reframed UAP as potential national security risks.

Key institutional milestones:
- **UAP Task Force** established August 2020
- **ODNI Preliminary Assessment** (June 25, 2021): 144 reports from 2004-2021 analyzed
- **[AARO](../entities/aaro.md)** established July 2022 to centralize investigations
- **July 26, 2023 [Congressional Hearing](../events/congressional-hearings.md)**: David Grusch alleged multi-decade retrieval programs; David Fravor and Ryan Graves testified on encounters
- **AARO March 2024 Historical Review**: Found no evidence of extraterrestrial involvement or government cover-ups
- **November 2024 AARO Report**: 757 new incidents; 21 unresolved cases warranting further scrutiny
