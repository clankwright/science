# Catalog of UFO Religions

> **Summary:** This article catalogs notable UFO religious movements, from enduring organizations like the Aetherius Society and Raelism to defunct groups like Heaven's Gate and Chen Tao, as well as borderline classifications such as Scientology, the Nation of Islam, and Falun Gong. Entries include founders, founding dates, core doctrines, geographic concentrations, membership estimates, and key controversies.

## Enduring and Active Movements

### Aetherius Society

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **Founded** | 1955, London, United Kingdom |
| **Founder** | George King (1919 to 1997) |
| **Core doctrine** | Communications from 408 extraterrestrial "Cosmic Masters" (including reinterpreted figures of Jesus and Buddha) residing on planets such as Venus, Saturn, and Jupiter. Service to humanity as the paramount spiritual practice. |
| **Origin narrative** | King, a former taxi driver who practiced yoga 8 to 10 hours daily since the mid-1940s, claimed to receive a telepathic "Command" on May 8, 1954, from a Venusian entity named Aetherius, instructing him as the "Primary Terrestrial Mental Channel" for an Interplanetary Parliament. |
| **Key practices** | "King Yoga" (intellectual truth-seeking, meditation, selfless service); Operation Starlight (charging 19 designated holy mountains with spiritual energy); Operation Prayer Power (creating and activating prayer batteries to store and release prana). |
| **Membership** | A few hundred active members as of 2018, primarily in Los Angeles and London. Low-cost membership (e.g., US$60 annually in North America). |
| **Status** | Active. Maintains temples in the UK and US; offers online services, live-streamed events, audiobooks, and master classes. |

### International Raelian Movement

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **Founded** | 1974, France |
| **Founder** | Claude Vorilhon (born September 30, 1946), who adopted the name Rael |
| **Core doctrine** | Humanity was scientifically created by an advanced race called the Elohim through genetic engineering approximately 25,000 years ago. Biblical figures like Yahweh and Jesus were Elohim emissaries. Atheistic faith rejecting traditional theism. |
| **Origin narrative** | Vorilhon reported his first contact with an Elohim being on December 13, 1973, near Clermont-Ferrand, France. A second encounter occurred in 1975 at the Puy de Lassolas volcano. |
| **Key practices** | "Sensual meditation" (non-reproductive sexual practices for pleasure and enlightenment); hierarchical structure of "guides" advancing through six levels; annual seminars and "happiness academies." |
| **Symbol** | Combines a swastika (interpreted as representing infinity and genetic engineering) with a Star of David. Has drawn criticism for evoking Nazi imagery; Rael disavows such associations. |
| **Clonaid controversy** | In 1997, Rael established Clonaid, a for-profit company aimed at human reproductive cloning. On December 26, 2002, Clonaid announced the birth of "Eve," purportedly the first cloned human. No verifiable DNA evidence or independent verification was ever produced. Scientists and regulators dismissed the claims as unsubstantiated or fraudulent. |
| **Political philosophy** | Proposes "geniocracy," governance by individuals with exceptional intelligence. |
| **Membership** | Rael claims around 100,000 adherents globally (concentrated in France, Canada, Japan). Independent assessments suggest lower active participation, potentially in the thousands. As of 2025, Rael resides in Japan. |
| **Status** | Active. Plans for a 50th anniversary gathering in 2025. Continues promoting an embassy for extraterrestrials with architectural plans unveiled in 2022. The French government has classified Raelism as a potentially antisocial sect. |

### Unarius Academy of Science

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **Founded** | 1954, Los Angeles, California |
| **Founders** | Ernest L. Norman and Ruth E. Norman |
| **Core doctrine** | "Interdimensional science" emphasizing psychic development, reincarnation, and communication with extraterrestrial intelligences to resolve past-life karma. |
| **Key works** | Ernest Norman's *The Truth About Mars* (1956), detailing spirit communications from Martian entities. |
| **Key practices** | Past-life regression therapy; production of educational films on UFO phenomena, telepathy, and spiritual healing. |
| **Failed prophecy** | 1997 prediction of 33 spacecraft landing in El Cajon, California, which did not materialize. The group reframed this as focusing on personal spiritual evolution over literal eschatology. |
| **Membership** | Estimated in the low hundreds as of the late 1990s. |
| **Status** | Active as a nonprofit. Operates from El Cajon, California, conducting classes, virtual tours, and conclaves. |

### Ashtar Command

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **Founded** | Early 1950s (channeled messages); formalized through publications in 1970s |
| **Key figures** | George Van Tassel (received telepathic messages from Ashtar beginning 1952); Tuella (published *Project: World Evacuation* in 1974) |
| **Core doctrine** | A hierarchical interstellar fleet led by Ashtar (or Ashtar Sheran), an ascended being overseeing Earth's spiritual ascension and defense against negative forces. Emphasizes non-physical evacuation of select humans during cataclysms. |

| **Status** | Active via online platforms for prophecies of alien intervention. No centralized organization. |

## Defunct or Marginalized Groups

### Heaven's Gate

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **Founded** | 1974 (initially as Human Individual Metamorphosis) |
| **Founders** | Marshall Herff Applewhite Jr. (born May 17, 1932) and Bonnie Lu Nettles (born July 18, 1927). Met in Houston, Texas, 1972. Known as "Do" and "Ti." |
| **Core doctrine** | The "Next Level" or "Evolutionary Level Above Human" (TELAH): an androgynous, non-corporeal existence achievable only by overcoming "human mammalian" traits (sexuality, ego, familial bonds). Physical death as necessary "shedding" of the body for UFO transport to the Next Level. Fused Christian apocalypticism (interpreting biblical "clouds" as spacecraft) with New Age concepts. |
| **Practices** | Ascetic communalism: celibacy, uniform appearances, renunciation of personal possessions and identities. Voluntary castration for some male members. Operated Higher Source, a web design firm, to fund operations in the 1990s. |
| **Peak membership** | Approximately 200 after a 1975 Waldport, Oregon meeting. Declined to fewer than 50 by the 1990s. |
| **Nettles' death** | Died of liver cancer on January 19, 1985. Applewhite framed this as her successful "demonstration" of ascension. |
| **Mass suicide** | March 22 to 26, 1997: 39 members (21 females, 18 males, aged 26 to 72) executed a staged suicide in shifts at a leased mansion in Rancho Santa Fe, California. Each ingested 10 to 30 grams of phenobarbital dissolved in applesauce or pudding, chased with vodka, then affixed plastic bags secured with elastic bands. All wore identical black-and-white uniforms, purple armbands, and black Nike Decades sneakers. Pre-recorded farewell videos asserted the act as "graduation." Applewhite was among the deceased. Autopsies confirmed no evidence of external coercion. |
| **Status** | Defunct. The heavensgate.com website is maintained by former members. |

### Chen Tao (God's Salvation Church)

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **Founded** | 1993, Taiwan |
| **Founder** | Hon-Ming Chen, former mental health professional |
| **Core doctrine** | Blended UFO contact, apocalyptic prophecies, and elements of Buddhism, Christianity, and Taoism. Anticipated God's physical arrival via UFO and public television broadcast on March 31, 1998. |
| **Failed prophecy** | Over 100 members relocated to Garland, Texas, in preparation for the March 1998 event, which did not occur. |
| **Membership** | Approximately 2,000 adherents by the mid-1990s. |
| **Status** | Effectively defunct by the early 2000s. Chen fled authorities amid fraud allegations; the movement fragmented into inactivity. |

### Order of the Solar Temple

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **Founded** | 1984 |
| **Founders** | Joseph Di Mambro and Luc Jouret |
| **Core doctrine** | Syncretic framework blending Templar revivalism, Rosicrucian esotericism, New Age spirituality, UFO/alien contact beliefs, and apocalyptic warnings of environmental collapse. Transit to Sirius via extraterrestrial means. |
| **Deaths** | Between 1994 and 1997, over 70 members died in orchestrated suicides and murders across Switzerland, France, and Canada. Initial events (October 1994): 53 individuals, many shot or asphyxiated before structures were set ablaze, framed as voluntary "transit." Additional waves in 1995 and 1997 added 21 more fatalities. Investigations attributed many to orchestrated murders disguised as mass suicides. |
| **Status** | Defunct. |

## Borderline or Controversial Classifications

The following movements incorporate UFO or alien themes but are debated as to whether they qualify as "UFO religions" proper, since extraterrestrial elements may be subsidiary to therapeutic, ethnic, or syncretic frameworks.

### Scientology

L. Ron Hubbard's Operating Thetan III materials (c. 1967) detail a galactic overlord Xenu's transport of billions of beings to Earth 75 million years ago via spacecraft, followed by hydrogen bomb detonations and soul clustering. Some researchers (including Andreas Grunschloss) categorize it among UFO religions due to these space opera narratives. Others contend it diverges by lacking messianic alien saviors or apocalyptic saucer landings; Hubbard's science fiction background (including the 1982 novel *Battlefield Earth*) further complicates classification.

### Nation of Islam

Founded by Wallace Fard Muhammad in 1930 and expanded by Elijah Muhammad, the Nation of Islam incorporates the "Mother Plane" or "Wheel in the Middle of a Wheel," a colossal, human-built craft described as 1,500 miles in diameter, composed of 150 smaller planes, prophesied to execute divine judgment by raining destruction on oppressors. The concept draws from Ezekiel 1:15-21. Louis Farrakhan reported a 1975 vision of this entity. Though it parallels UFO religions in positing advanced technology, the NOI's primary orientation is toward black nationalist Islam, racial mythology (e.g., Yakub's creation of whites 6,600 years ago), and socioeconomic empowerment.

### Falun Gong

Founded by Li Hongzhi in Changchun, China, on May 13, 1992. Posits shape-shifting aliens from other dimensions who genetically manipulate humans to erode morality, countered by practitioners' cultivation of virtue through qigong exercises and meditation. Li's lectures (*Zhuan Falun*, 1994) claim UFOs as vehicles for interdimensional beings. However, core practices emphasize energy refinement and karmic purification akin to Buddhist-Taoist traditions, relegating extraterrestrials to explanatory side-notes rather than salvific agents. Persecuted by Chinese authorities since 1999.

## Leon Festinger and the "Seekers" Study

A foundational case in the sociology of UFO religions is Leon Festinger's 1956 study of a Chicago-based group ("the Seekers") led by Dorothy Martin, who predicted cataclysmic flooding on December 21, 1954, with a UFO rescue of believers. When the prophecy failed, members did not abandon their beliefs but instead intensified recruitment efforts, rationalizing that their faith had averted the disaster. Festinger's *When Prophecy Fails* (1956) established the theory of cognitive dissonance as it applies to apocalyptic movements, a dynamic that has recurred across multiple UFO religions.
