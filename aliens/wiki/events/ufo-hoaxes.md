# UFO-Related Hoaxes

> **Summary:** UFO hoaxes encompass deliberate fabrications of sightings, photographs, physical traces, or encounters purporting to demonstrate unidentified flying objects or extraterrestrial visitation. These deceptions, motivated by notoriety, financial gain, or psychological factors, have proliferated since the 19th century. Their exposure through forensic analysis, perpetrator confessions, and replication via conventional means underscores the necessity of rigorous evidence-based verification in UAP research. This article catalogs major confirmed hoaxes chronologically, from the 1835 Great Moon Hoax through modern CGI and AI fabrications.

## 19th Century

### The Great Moon Hoax (1835)

The *New York Sun* published a series of articles in August 1835 falsely attributing discoveries of lunar civilization to astronomer John Herschel. The fabrication, written by reporter Richard Adams Locke, described bat-winged humanoids, blue unicorns, and temples on the Moon, boosting the paper's circulation before exposure. It established a template for exploiting scientific authority to lend credibility to sensational claims.

### Mystery Airship Wave (1896-1897)

Widespread reports across the United States described dirigible-like craft with lights, sometimes crewed by human inventors or extraterrestrial pilots. Investigations revealed most as journalist fabrications exploiting public fascination with emerging aviation during the era of yellow journalism.

Key confirmed hoaxes within the wave:
- **Aurora, Texas "crash" (April 17, 1897):** Reporter S. E. Haydon claimed an airship exploded into a windmill, killing a diminutive pilot buried in the local cemetery. No debris, bodies, or fragments were ever recovered. Air Force inquiries confirmed it as fabricated to boost newspaper sales.
- **Hamilton's cow abduction (April 19, 1897):** Kansas farmer Alexander Hamilton claimed an airship abducted his cow; he later admitted it stemmed from a local "Liars' Club" contest.

The wave's cessation by May 1897 is attributed to exhausted novelty and debunkings. No patents, prototypes, or independent witnesses to claimed interactions were ever produced.

## Mid-20th Century Contactee Era

### George Adamski (1950s)

George Adamski (1891-1965), a Polish-born American occultist operating a hamburger stand near Mount Palomar, California, claimed physical contact with extraterrestrials from Venus, Mars, and Saturn beginning in the early 1950s.

**Claims:** On November 20, 1952, Adamski reported meeting a humanoid Venusian named "Orthon" in the desert near Desert Center, California. He documented this in *Flying Saucers Have Landed* (1953) and *Inside the Space Ships* (1955), describing tours of the Moon and meetings with "Space Brothers."

**Fabrication evidence:**
- One iconic "scout craft" photo was identified as a chicken egg incubator (brooder) modified with lights and an infrared bulb; microscopic analysis revealed studio floodlight reflections and fiber threads from model construction
- Wernher von Braun associate Walther Riedel examined the photos in 1953 and concluded they were hoaxes
- Ufologist James W. Moseley's 1957 *Saucer News* expose compiled witness discrepancies and physical evidence of deception
- [Project Blue Book](../events/project-blue-book.md) head Edward J. Ruppelt visited Adamski incognito and found his demonstrations unconvincing
- Adamski's claims of habitable Venus conflicted with emerging data on its 900 F surface temperature and sulfuric acid atmosphere
- Ex-wife and accomplice accounts were inconsistent

### 1967 British Flying Saucer Hoax

On September 4, 1967, apprentice engineers at the Royal Aircraft Establishment in Farnborough placed six fabricated "spaceships" across southern England to promote a student charity event (Rag Week).

**Construction:** Fiberglass shells coated in aluminum gel (~3 ft diameter), with battery-powered loudspeakers emitting humming sounds (mercury tilt switch activated) and flour-water mixture mimicking "alien residue."

**Deployment:** Six devices placed in a straight line spanning ~150 miles, from Clevedon (Somerset) to the Isle of Sheppey (Kent). Discovered that morning, they triggered police cordons, bomb disposal teams, an RAF helicopter airlift, and Ministry of Defence investigations. Media outlets including the *Daily Mirror* and *New York Times* ran headlines suggesting extraterrestrial incursion.

**Resolution:** Students confessed that evening; no prosecutions despite initial threats. The prank raised approximately 2,000 pounds for charity. UK UFO reports totaled 362 that year.

## Crashed UFO and Physical Evidence Hoaxes

### Alien Autopsy (1995)

British music promoter Ray Santilli released 17 minutes of black-and-white film purporting to show the dissection of an extraterrestrial body recovered from the 1947 [Roswell incident](../events/roswell.md).

**Claims:** Santilli stated the film originated from U.S. military archives, obtained during a 1993 search for Elvis Presley concert footage, where a cameraman sold him degraded 1947 reels.

**Broadcast:** Premiered at a London press conference on May 4, 1995. Fox Television's *Alien Autopsy: Fact or Fiction?* (August 28, 1995) drew 20 million U.S. viewers.

**Exposure:** Forensic pathologist John Altshuler and special effects experts identified anatomical errors, mismatched shadows, incorrect surgical techniques, and visible seams. In 2006, Santilli confessed in a documentary that the footage was a "reconstruction" using a dummy constructed from sheep brains, chicken intestines, and animal parts, filmed by effects artist John Humphreys in a London studio. Santilli maintained he had witnessed authentic 1947 material, but no evidence of this original has surfaced. Santilli earned millions from licensing deals.

### Berwyn Mountain "Welsh Roswell" (1974)

On January 23, 1974, residents near the Berwyn Mountains in north Wales reported an explosion, ground tremors, and bright lights. Nurse Pat Evans claimed to observe an elongated object with a red glow on the hillside.

**Explanation:** Ministry of Defence files (declassified 2010) attributed the events to:
- A magnitude 3.5 earthquake (epicenter ~10 miles southwest of Llandrillo, 9:28 PM)
- A bright fireball meteor visible across the UK (~10 PM)
- Five contemporaneous UFO sightings elsewhere in Britain, consistent with the meteor's visibility

No crash site, wreckage, or military retrieval evidence was found.

## Photographic Hoaxes

### Billy Meier (1975-1980+)

Eduard "Billy" Meier, a Swiss farmer, claimed over 1,000 photographs of "beamships" from Plejaran visitors, taken primarily 1975-1980 near his Schmidruti property.

**Fabrication evidence:**
- Enlargements revealed suspension strings, disproportionate shadows, and no atmospheric distortion expected from distant objects
- Independent investigators replicated identical images using miniature models (aluminum foil, plastic, household items) suspended by fishing line
- Ex-wife Kalliope Zafiris Meier provided sworn testimony confirming fabrication of models from scrap materials (yogurt lids, coat hangers)
- Metal samples claimed as beamship fragments were classified as conventional aluminum alloys (6061 series) with titanium dioxide coatings and etched patterns reproducible via standard acid immersion or anodizing

### Ilkley Moor "Alien" Photo (1987)

On December 1, 1987, Philip Spencer (pseudonym) claimed to photograph a humanoid entity on Ilkley Moor, West Yorkshire, England. The developed photo showed a blurry, shadowy silhouette. Hypnotic regression yielded abduction memories, though hypnosis is known to facilitate false memories. Kodak authenticated the negative as untampered but could not rule out a costumed person or propped object. No landing traces, radiation, or biological residue were documented.

### Gulf Breeze Sightings (1987-1988)

Ed Walters of Gulf Breeze, Florida claimed repeated UFO sightings supported by photographs. Models matching the alleged UFOs were subsequently recovered from his attic, confirming fabrication. (Limited detail in available sources; additional sourcing needed.)

## Modern Digital and Artifact Hoaxes

### CGI Fabrications

The proliferation of consumer-grade visual effects software has enabled increasingly convincing UFO video hoaxes:

- **2011 Jerusalem UFO:** Videos depicting a glowing orb hovering above the Dome of the Rock before ascending rapidly. Investigations identified synchronized audio added post-filming, and one "third angle" proven to be a digital fabrication derived from a static tourist photograph with artificially imposed motion.
- **2013 Tsirbas UFO Landing:** Greek-Canadian VFX artist Konstantinos Tsirbas created a disc landing near a parked car using Autodesk Maya and Nuke, later confirming it as a demonstration of photorealistic CGI. Garnered millions of views.
- **2023 Moon impact:** A clip of a UFO striking the lunar surface was identified as CGI from a 3D artist's portfolio, with uniform particle simulations absent in authentic astronomical recordings.
- **2024 Maryland orbs:** Three orbs near power lines traced by Reuters fact-checkers to AI tools like Midjourney, displaying synthetic textures and improbable light refraction.

### Drone-Based Hoaxes

Unmanned aerial vehicles with LED lights or attachments can replicate UFO maneuvers (hovering, sudden direction changes). Analyses of viral videos reveal drone signatures: FAA-compliant altitude limits, propeller noise spectra matching commercial DJI models. Specific confessions are rare, but staging has been inferred from pattern analysis.

### Jaime Maussan's "Alien Mummies" (2017-2023)

Mexican journalist Jaime Maussan has repeatedly promoted mummified remains as extraterrestrial evidence:

- **2017:** First showcased three-fingered "alien" mummies from Peru's Nazca region. Independent radiographic imaging revealed mismatched bone densities and evidence of manual assembly.
- **September 2023:** Presented two small desiccated "non-human" bodies to Mexico's Congress during a UFO hearing, asserting under oath they were ~1,000 years old with 30% unidentified DNA, three-fingered hands, metallic implants, and egg-like internal structures.

**Debunking:**
- January 2024: Peruvian forensic examinations of similar specimens confirmed construction from animal bones (birds, dogs), human fetal remains, and modern synthetic glues (epoxy)
- DNA sequencing yielded predominantly human genetic material; anomalies attributable to degradation or contamination
- Anthropologists noted the three-fingered design echoes pre-Columbian cultural artifacts but lacked verifiable provenance
- Proliferation linked to illicit grave robbing incentivized by "alien" speculation in Nazca's desiccating salt flats
- Maussan has a documented history of prior debunked UFO claims from the 1990s

### 2016 "Ancient Egyptian Alien Coin"

Claims of an Egyptian coin depicting an extraterrestrial were based on digitally altered images of a genuine Roman medallion from the Cabinet de Medailles in Paris. The original features portraits related to Emperor Hadrian's Antinous and Dionysus; one side was photoshopped to resemble a Grey alien.

## Patterns and Implications

UFO hoaxes share recurring characteristics:
- **Motivation:** Personal notoriety, financial gain, ideological commitment, or psychological suggestibility
- **Exploitation of ambiguity:** Low-quality images, remote locations, and classified contexts provide cover
- **Media amplification:** Sensational claims receive disproportionate coverage relative to subsequent retractions
- **Technological escalation:** Each era's hoaxes leverage available technology (photography, film, CGI, AI, drones)
- **Damage to legitimate research:** Hoaxes undermine credibility of genuine anomalous observations and institutional [identification studies](../concepts/identification-studies.md)

Government agencies have also engaged in deliberate [UFO disinformation](../entities/area-51.md) to protect classified programs, with a 2024 Pentagon review documenting military units fabricating UFO evidence to mask weapons testing.
