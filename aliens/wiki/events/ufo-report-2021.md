# UFO Report: ODNI Preliminary Assessment (2021)

> **Summary:** The *Preliminary Assessment: Unidentified Aerial Phenomena*, released by the Office of the Director of National Intelligence on June 25, 2021, was the first unclassified U.S. intelligence community assessment of UAP. It analyzed 144 reports from U.S. military sources spanning November 2004 to March 2021, with 80 corroborated by multiple sensors. Only one case was resolved (a deflating balloon). Eighteen incidents featured anomalous behaviors including apparent near-instantaneous acceleration, hypersonic velocities without sonic booms, and stationary positioning against prevailing winds. The report proposed five explanatory categories, found no evidence of extraterrestrial origins, and attributed unresolved cases to insufficient data rather than extraordinary claims.

## Legislative Mandate

The Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, in its report accompanying the Intelligence Authorization Act for FY2021 (S. Rept. 116-270, June 17, 2020), directed the ODNI to produce an unclassified intelligence community assessment on UAP within 180 days. The National Defense Authorization Act for FY2021 (P.L. 116-283, enacted January 1, 2021), Section 1211, formalized this requirement, mandating:

- A detailed analysis of UAP data and intelligence possessed by the federal government
- Descriptions of interagency coordination frameworks for UAP data handling
- Identification of analytical gaps and responsible offices
- Evaluations of risks to national security, flight safety, and operations in air, space, and maritime domains
- A classified annex for sensitive details
- Implementation within 60 days of a secure reporting process for military UAP sightings

The mandate reflected bipartisan congressional concern over UAP incursions into military training ranges and designated airspace.

## UAP Task Force

The Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force (UAPTF) was established August 4, 2020, by Deputy Secretary of Defense David L. Norquist. Led primarily by the Navy, it coordinated with the Air Force, FAA, and FBI. Data collection relied on existing sensors (radar, infrared, electro-optical) supplemented by visual observations and standardized reporting protocols initiated by the Navy in March 2019 and the Air Force in November 2020.

Significant limitations:
- Sociocultural stigma historically deterred comprehensive reporting among aviators
- Sensor instrumentation was not optimized for UAP detection
- Pre-2019 data was decentralized and fragmented
- Only 80 of 144 cases had multi-sensor corroboration

## Scope and Data

The assessment covered 144 reports from U.S. Government sources, November 2004 to March 2021. Of these, 143 post-dated late 2017, reflecting the post-disclosure reporting surge. Data sources were limited to verifiable military encounters; civilian reports were excluded.

Key statistics:
- **144** total reports
- **80** corroborated by multiple sensors (radar, IR, electro-optical, weapon seekers, visual)
- **18** incidents (across 21 reports) with unusual movement patterns
- **11** reported near-misses with U.S. aircraft
- **1** resolved case (deflating balloon)
- **143** unresolved

## Key Observations

In the 18 anomalous incidents, observers reported:
- UAP remaining stationary in winds aloft
- Movement against prevailing winds
- Abrupt maneuvers without discernible propulsion
- High speeds without exhaust plumes or sonic booms
- Apparent trans-medium capabilities (air-to-sea transitions)
- Apparent jamming or spoofing of military sensors

Many sightings clustered near U.S. military training ranges and assets, possibly reflecting enhanced detection opportunities rather than anomalous targeting.

## Five Explanatory Categories

The UAPTF proposed five provisional bins:

1. **Airborne Clutter:** Birds, balloons, UAVs, debris generating false positives
2. **Natural Atmospheric Phenomena:** Ice crystals, atmospheric refraction, plasma formations from temperature inversions
3. **U.S. Government or Industry Developmental Programs:** Classified domestic testing of advanced aircraft, propulsion, or EW prototypes
4. **Foreign Adversary Systems:** Surveillance platforms, hypersonic weapons, or adversarial UAVs from nation-states
5. **Other:** Residual category for 11 cases with unusual multi-sensor data but stemming from evidentiary gaps rather than confirmed exotic origins

The framework explicitly avoided presuming extraterrestrial involvement, recommending data-driven adjudication.

## Findings

- No empirical evidence linked UAP to extraterrestrial activity or confirmed foreign intelligence efforts
- Data limitations precluded attributing causes like breakthrough propulsion or sensor vulnerabilities
- Foreign adversary systems (potentially China or Russia) remained a viable explanation for select UAP demonstrating advanced capabilities
- Many anomalies aligned with sensor artifacts, electronic warfare spoofing, or perceptual errors under high-stress conditions
- The intelligence community maintained an agnostic posture, prioritizing verifiable data over speculative narratives

## National Security and Safety Assessment

- UAP incursions into restricted airspace could enable undetected surveillance of defense capabilities
- 11 near-miss incidents involved objects at altitudes of 45,000-80,000 ft and ranges of 100-200 nautical miles from military assets
- Pilots reported avoiding training areas, degrading readiness
- The clustering near sensitive assets raised counterintelligence concerns

## Classified Annex

A classified annex accompanied the public report, drawing from geospatial intelligence, signals intelligence, human intelligence, and measurement data. The annex did not modify the unclassified assessment's conclusion that available evidence does not support extraterrestrial explanations. Classification was necessitated by protection of intelligence sources and collection methods.

## Reception

### Government Response

Pentagon Press Secretary John Kirby stated the DoD takes UAP seriously as a domain awareness issue. Deputy Defense Secretary Kathleen Hicks issued a same-day memorandum directing standardized UAP reporting procedures with incident summaries due within two weeks. Senators Marco Rubio and Mark Warner welcomed the report as foundational for ongoing scrutiny, advocating budget allocations for advanced sensors.

### Scientific and Skeptical Views

- Physicists noted that reported anomalous speeds and accelerations often stem from parallax effects, IR glare, or sensor artifacts
- SETI Institute astronomers argued interstellar origins are improbable given energy requirements under relativity
- Video analyst Mick West replicated many associated UAP videos using commercial software to demonstrate mundane causes
- Critics noted the limited sample size and absence of peer-reviewed analysis
- The scientific consensus viewed the "unidentified" category as an invitation for empirical scrutiny, not validation of non-human intelligence

### Extraterrestrial Hypothesis Advocacy

Luis Elizondo (former [AATIP](../events/aatip.md) director) interpreted the 18 anomalous incidents as indicative of technology beyond known human capabilities, emphasizing acceleration without visible exhaust, hypersonic velocities without sonic booms, and apparent signature management. These interpretations lack substantiation through physical evidence; the report identified no recovered artifacts or biologics attributable to extraterrestrial sources.

## Subsequent Actions

### AARO Formation

The [All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office](../entities/aaro.md) (AARO) was established July 20, 2022, succeeding the UAPTF. By 2024, AARO had analyzed over 1,000 total cases, consistently resolving ~90% as mundane phenomena (commercial drones, weather balloons, airborne clutter). AARO's March 2024 Historical Record Report (Volume 1, covering 1945-2023) found no verifiable records of crash retrievals, reverse-engineering programs, or non-human technology.
