# Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP)

> **Summary:** The Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP) was a Defense Intelligence Agency program established in 2007 with approximately $22 million allocated through 2012 to study anomalous aerospace phenomena. Directed by Luis Elizondo, AATIP gathered data from military sensor systems on objects exhibiting hypersonic speeds, sudden maneuvers defying known aerodynamics, and transmedium capabilities without visible propulsion. The program's existence was revealed by a December 16, 2017 New York Times article that also released three Navy videos (FLIR, Gimbal, GoFast), marking a paradigm shift that reframed UFOs from fringe speculation to national security imperatives and led directly to the UAP Task Force, the 2021 ODNI report, and AARO.

## Establishment and Funding

AATIP was established in 2007 under the Defense Intelligence Agency, with approximately $22 million allocated through 2012 to study anomalous aerospace phenomena. The program focused on gathering data from military sensor systems, including radar, infrared, and electro-optical recordings of objects exhibiting characteristics beyond known technology.

The program's existence was not publicly acknowledged during its operational period. Following official funding, the program reportedly continued in some capacity with reduced resources.

## Focus Areas

AATIP investigated:
- Objects exhibiting hypersonic speeds without visible propulsion signatures
- Sudden maneuvers defying known aerodynamics
- Transmedium capabilities (air-to-water transitions)
- Objects evading detection by advanced sensor systems

The program emphasized empirical threat assessment, framing UAP as potential adversarial technologies or unknown aerospace phenomena rather than extraterrestrial encounters.

## Key Cases Investigated

### USS Nimitz Encounter (November 2004)

The pivotal case investigated by AATIP was the November 2004 encounter involving the USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group off the California coast. F/A-18 pilots, including Commander David Fravor, visually observed and pursued a white, Tic Tac-shaped object approximately 40 feet long that demonstrated:
- Rapid acceleration
- Instantaneous direction changes
- Descent from 80,000 feet to sea level in seconds

The encounter was corroborated by ship-based radar and captured on FLIR (Forward-Looking Infrared) video. See [USS Nimitz 2004 Encounter](../events/uss-nimitz-2004.md) for detailed analysis.

### East Coast Incidents (2014-2015)

Navy pilots off the U.S. East Coast reported similar incursions from 2014 to 2015, captured in the Gimbal and GoFast videos showing rotating objects with extreme velocities and no apparent wings or exhaust. Skeptical analysts have offered alternative explanations:
- **Gimbal:** The object's apparent rotation may result from the infrared camera's gimbal mechanism slewing to track a distant conventional aircraft, with engine glare mimicking a saucer shape
- **GoFast:** The object's apparent high speed may be a parallax illusion from a stationary or slow-moving object (e.g., a balloon) viewed from a rapidly moving observer aircraft through a narrow 0.7-degree field of view

## Luis Elizondo

Luis Elizondo directed AATIP's operations until his resignation in October 2017. He resigned citing excessive secrecy and internal resistance to investigating UAP. Elizondo argued that AATIP's data demonstrated the reality of objects outperforming U.S. aircraft in speed, stealth, and maneuverability.

Following his departure, Elizondo became a prominent public advocate for UAP disclosure, emphasizing that 18 incidents in the subsequent [2021 ODNI report](../events/ufo-report-2021.md) demonstrated technology beyond known human capabilities. He interpreted characteristics like acceleration without visible exhaust, hypersonic velocities without sonic booms, and apparent signature management as indicative of non-human technology.

Critics note that Elizondo's extraterrestrial interpretations lack substantiation through physical evidence. The [2021 ODNI report](../events/ufo-report-2021.md) he advocated for identified no recovered artifacts or biologics attributable to extraterrestrial sources, and AARO's subsequent investigations resolved the vast majority of cases as mundane phenomena.

## The 2017 New York Times Disclosure

On December 16, 2017, the *New York Times* published "Glowing Auras and 'Black Money': The Pentagon's Mysterious U.F.O. Program," revealing AATIP's existence and releasing three Navy videos (FLIR, Gimbal, GoFast). This disclosure, driven by Elizondo's whistleblowing:

- Reframed UFOs (later termed UAP) from fringe speculation to national security imperatives
- Prompted congressional inquiries and Pentagon acknowledgments of unexplained events near sensitive sites
- Shifted emphasis from debunking toward empirical threat assessment
- Led directly to establishment of the UAP Task Force (June 2020) and the 2021 ODNI preliminary assessment

The Department of Defense officially confirmed the authenticity of the three videos on April 27, 2020.

## Legacy and Institutional Successors

AATIP's work was continued and expanded through:

1. **UAP Task Force (UAPTF):** Established August 4, 2020 under ODNI/DoD
2. **2021 ODNI Preliminary Assessment:** Analyzed 144 UAP reports; see [UFO Report 2021](../events/ufo-report-2021.md)
3. **[AARO](../entities/aaro.md):** Established July 20, 2022 as a centralized investigation office

AATIP's significance lies less in its operational findings (which remain largely classified) than in its role as the catalyst for institutional legitimization of UAP as a national security topic, ending decades of stigma that followed [Project Blue Book](../events/project-blue-book.md)'s closure in 1969.
