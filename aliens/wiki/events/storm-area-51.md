# Storm Area 51

> **Summary:** "Storm Area 51, They Can't Stop All of Us" was a viral Facebook event created on June 27, 2019 by Matty Roberts, a 20-year-old college student from Bakersfield, California. Intended as a joke, it amassed over 2 million RSVPs calling for a mass incursion into Area 51 on September 20, 2019. Actual attendance was roughly 1,500 people across festival sites in Rachel and Hiko, Nevada, with about 75 people gathering at the base's back gate. No perimeter breaches occurred. The event prompted emergency declarations, $250,000 in local costs, approximately $11 million in federal security spending, and spawned the Alienstock music festival. It remains a case study in the gap between internet virality and real-world mobilization.

## Origins

On June 27, 2019, Matty Roberts created a public Facebook event titled "Storm Area 51, They Can't Stop All of Us." Roberts later stated the post was made impulsively late at night as entertainment for his gaming community. The event description humorously proposed converging at the Area 51 Alien Center tourist attraction near Rachel, Nevada on September 20, 2019, and rushing the gates en masse, with absurd tactical suggestions including adopting the "Naruto run" from anime to evade guards.

Within days the event garnered thousands of RSVPs, exceeding 1 million "going" responses by mid-July 2019 and surpassing 2 million by early August. Celebrity engagement amplified the phenomenon: rapper Lil Nas X tweeted support, and photoshopped endorsements from figures like Elon Musk circulated widely.

## Meme Evolution

The event generated derivative content across Reddit and Twitter: image macros of SpongeBob SquarePants crowds storming gates, edited NASA photos implying alien reveals, and parody events like "Storm Area 52" and "Storm the DMV." The U.S. Air Force itself entered the discourse with a Garfield meme warning of arrests, which users remixed into further viral content. Merchandise sales proliferated with "Storm Area 51" apparel from independent sellers.

## Official Responses

### Federal and Military

On July 15, 2019, the Air Force publicly acknowledged awareness, with spokesperson Laura Lundelius emphasizing the installation was an active training range with "very quick and very loud aircraft" and armed security authorized to use deadly force. Additional security forces were deployed, barricades erected, and coordination established with the FBI and local law enforcement. A September 21, 2019 DoD tweet featuring a B-2 Spirit bomber and text suggesting aerial strikes against crowds was deleted with an apology the following day.

### Local Government

Lincoln County declared a state of emergency on August 21, 2019 (unanimous County Commission vote) to access state resources. Sheriff Kerry Lee anticipated up to $30,000 in overtime. Preparations included traffic control, medical response teams, and over 300 first responders from Nevada Highway Patrol, Las Vegas Metro Police, and state emergency management. Advisories warned of extreme desert conditions (100+ F days, sub-freezing nights) and the town of Rachel's population of 54 residents with minimal infrastructure.

## September 2019 Events

### Gate Gathering (September 20)

Approximately 75 individuals gathered at the base's back gate. Two were immediately detained: one woman attempting to duck under the gate, one man urinating nearby. Most participants engaged in photography, informal demonstrations, and meme-inspired antics like the Naruto run. The crowd dispersed by late afternoon without incident.

### Alienstock Festival (September 20-22)

Originally organized by Roberts in Rachel, he withdrew on September 11, citing inadequate infrastructure and risk of a "humanitarian crisis." Connie West, proprietor of the Little A'Le'Inn, hosted a scaled-down version on ~30 acres, featuring continuous music noon to midnight, local vendors, and informal camping. Attendance estimates: organizers claimed 8,000-10,000 visitors over the weekend; on-site peaks were around 1,500.

### Storm Area 51 Basecamp (September 20-21)

Organized at the Alien Research Center in Hiko, Nevada (~40 miles east of Rachel), with day passes sold for $51 via Ticketmaster. Attendance fell far short of the 5,000 capacity: reports estimated only dozens on opening day. The second day was canceled due to insufficient participation.

### Security Incidents

- **September 12:** Two Dutch nationals (Ties Granzier and Govert Sweep) arrested three miles inside the Nevada Test and Training Range with drone equipment; they later pleaded guilty to trespassing
- **September 20:** Five total arrests by Lincoln County Sheriff: three for trespassing, one for indecent exposure, one for illegal camping
- **Weekend total:** Seven arrests across all events, primarily alcohol-related (DUI, disorderly conduct)
- No perimeter breaches occurred; no coordinated incursions attempted

## Economic Impact

- **Lincoln County costs:** ~$250,000 ($200,000 cleanup, overtime for 300 additional officers)
- **Federal security:** ~$11 million in enhanced measures at Nellis Air Force Base
- **Little A'Le'Inn:** $40,000 deficit from unmet sponsorship commitments
- **2025 lawsuit:** West secured a $3.4 million judgment against Roberts for breach of contract related to Alienstock planning disruptions
- **Net assessment:** Negligible economic gains for the region; preparatory and remedial costs far exceeded transient commercial activity

## Legacy

The event demonstrated the effectiveness of layered security at classified installations while exposing the gap between internet virality and real-world action. Netflix's 2025 documentary series *Trainwreck: Storm Area 51* examined the escalation from joke to military preparedness scenario.

Policy implications included reinforced protocols for monitoring social media to anticipate crowd energy and channel it toward permitted events, reducing unauthorized proximity to bases. The event validated existing access restrictions while revealing vulnerabilities in rural emergency response capacity for internet-driven phenomena.
