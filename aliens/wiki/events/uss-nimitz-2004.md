# USS Nimitz Encounter (2004)

> **Summary:** In November 2004, the USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group encountered an unidentified object off the California coast that became the most extensively documented military UAP case in the public record. F/A-18 pilots, including Commander David Fravor, visually observed and pursued a white, Tic Tac-shaped object approximately 40 feet long that demonstrated rapid acceleration, instantaneous direction changes, and apparent descent from 80,000 feet to sea level in seconds. The encounter was corroborated by ship-based radar and captured on FLIR video. The footage was among three Navy videos (FLIR, Gimbal, GoFast) released via the 2017 New York Times disclosure of AATIP and officially confirmed by the DoD in April 2020. Skeptical analysts have proposed conventional explanations for some video characteristics.

## The Encounter

In November 2004, the USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group was operating off the coast of California when ship-based radar systems detected anomalous tracks. F/A-18 Super Hornet pilots were vectored to investigate.

### Commander Fravor's Account

Commander David Fravor and his wingman visually observed a white, Tic Tac-shaped object approximately 40 feet in length, hovering over a disturbance on the ocean surface. According to Fravor's account:

- The object had no visible wings, rotors, exhaust plumes, or control surfaces
- When Fravor descended toward it, the object ascended to meet him, then accelerated away
- It demonstrated instantaneous direction changes
- Ship-based radar tracked it descending from approximately 80,000 feet to sea level in seconds
- It subsequently appeared at Fravor's pre-designated CAP (Combat Air Patrol) point, approximately 60 miles away, within seconds

### FLIR Video

A subsequent flight captured the object on Forward-Looking Infrared (FLIR) video, showing a white oblong shape against an ocean background. This footage, designated the "FLIR1" video, became one of three Navy UAP videos central to public discourse.

## The Three Navy Videos

The Nimitz FLIR footage was released alongside two other Navy UAP videos:

### Gimbal (2015)

Captured off the U.S. East Coast, the Gimbal video shows an object appearing to rotate while traveling against the wind. Skeptical analysis by Mick West and others proposes:
- The apparent rotation results from the infrared camera's gimbal mechanism slewing to track a distant conventional aircraft
- Engine glare from the aircraft mimics a saucer-like shape against a cold sky background

### GoFast (2015)

Also from the East Coast, GoFast shows an object apparently skimming the ocean at high speed. Skeptical analysis proposes:
- The apparent speed and low altitude result from parallax illusion
- A stationary or slow-moving object (possibly a balloon) appears fast and low relative to the rapidly moving observer aircraft
- The pod's narrow 0.7-degree field of view exaggerates relative motion

## Public Release Timeline

- **December 16, 2017:** The New York Times published the article revealing [AATIP](../events/aatip.md)'s existence and released the three videos
- **April 27, 2020:** The Department of Defense officially confirmed the videos' authenticity and released them through official channels
- **June 25, 2021:** The [ODNI Preliminary Assessment](../events/ufo-report-2021.md) included these encounters among its 144 analyzed cases

## Analysis and Debate

### In Favor of Anomalous Interpretation

- Multi-sensor corroboration (visual, radar, FLIR) strengthens the case against simple misidentification
- Commander Fravor and other trained military aviators consistently describe the object's performance as exceeding known technology
- Ship radar tracked objects at altitudes and speeds inconsistent with known aircraft or natural phenomena
- Luis Elizondo and [AATIP](../events/aatip.md) investigators highlighted the encounter as demonstrating technology beyond U.S. or adversary capabilities

### Skeptical Positions

- Physicist analyses note that reported characteristics (hypersonic speeds without sonic booms, instantaneous acceleration) often stem from misinterpretations of parallax effects, IR glare, or sensor artifacts
- Video analyst Mick West has replicated visual characteristics of the Gimbal and GoFast videos using commercial software, demonstrating mundane explanations
- The [2021 ODNI report](../events/ufo-report-2021.md) attributed unresolved cases to insufficient data rather than confirmed anomalous technology
- No physical evidence (debris, materials) has been recovered

### Kinematic Analysis

The reported kinematics (if taken at face value) would involve:
- Acceleration forces far exceeding human physiological tolerance
- Energy requirements inconsistent with known propulsion systems
- Absence of expected physical effects (sonic booms, thermal signatures, plasma generation)

These characteristics place the Nimitz encounter at the center of the UAP debate: either the observations represent genuinely anomalous technology, or they result from the compounding of perceptual errors, sensor artifacts, and interpretive frameworks applied to ambiguous data.

## Significance

The Nimitz encounter is the single most cited case in modern UAP discourse. It served as:
- The catalyst for AATIP's investigation and subsequent public disclosure
- A primary exhibit in the 2017 NYT article that triggered institutional re-engagement with UAP
- A foundational case in the [2021 ODNI report](../events/ufo-report-2021.md)
- A key reference point in [congressional hearings](../events/congressional-hearings.md) on UAP
