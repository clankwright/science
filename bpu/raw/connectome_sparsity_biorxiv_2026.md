# Non-random brain connectome wiring enables robust and efficient neural network function under high sparsity

**Venue:** bioRxiv (preprint), 2026.03.30.715411
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.03.30.715411v1

## Summary (from abstract / metadata)

Echo State Networks (reservoir computing) are wired directly from the adult
*Drosophila melanogaster* connectome (~140K neurons, ~50M synapses scale) and
compared against sparsity-matched random-wiring controls. The study isolates
why connectome topology outperforms random wiring specifically in the
high-sparsity regime that biological networks operate in.

## Key results

- Connectome-based networks (CoNNs) evaluated on 8 cognitive tasks show a
  narrowly distributed per-neuron task engagement, versus a broad spread in
  random networks.
- Per-neuron task importance correlates with node degree, local clustering
  coefficient, and self-recurrency; these correlations are significantly
  stronger in CoNNs than in random networks.
- The non-random topology yields more robust and efficient function
  specifically under high sparsity, not at dense connectivity.

## Relevance

Mechanistic account of the connectome-vs-random advantage at extreme sparsity,
linking graph-theoretic node features to per-neuron functional importance.
Complements connectome-reservoir-computing (FlyWire echo-state reservoirs) and
the broader connectome-as-architecture thesis.
